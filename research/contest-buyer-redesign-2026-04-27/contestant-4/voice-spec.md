# Voice Spec — Contestant 4

## The Voice: Compliance Specialist at the Counter

Think of the person who works at a specialized parts counter — someone who knows exactly which part you need, knows what breaks without it, and hands it to you without a lecture. Not condescending, not academic. Confident, specific, product-forward. The caller says "I need to comply with Colorado's AI law by June 30." The counter person says: "Eight documents. Built from the statute. $449. Download in five minutes." That's the voice.

This is not the Pragmatic Realist voice from the blog style guide. That voice is an educator helping the reader understand what a law means. This voice is a specialist helping the buyer get what they owe, now. The distinction is not tone (both are direct, no jargon) — it's orientation. The educator faces the law. The specialist faces the buyer.

---

## One-Paragraph Description

The marketing voice is short, declarative, and offer-shaped. It names the law, names the deadline or enforcement status, names the price, and names what you get — in that order, in the fewest words that carry the meaning. It does not explain what AI compliance is or why state laws passed. It assumes the buyer already knows they have a problem. It speaks to outcome ("your compliance, documented") not process ("templates built from enacted statute text"). It earns credibility through specificity (law name, section number, penalty amount, deadline date) not through credential-stacking or testimonials. It is the voice of a place that has exactly what you need and isn't going to waste your time.

---

## The Two Modes

The voice has two urgency registers, not one. Apply the right mode based on the law's status.

**Mode 1 — Deadline Approaching** (Colorado SB 24-205, laws with future effective dates)
Frame: You have a window. Use it.
Register: Specific countdown, forward pressure.
Example shape: "[Law name]. [Deadline date]. [What you need]. [Price]. Ready now."

**Mode 2 — Already Exposed** (NYC Local Law 144, Illinois HB3773, Texas TRAIGA — all in force)
Frame: The deadline passed. The exposure is current. Documentation is your defense.
Register: Calm, non-panicked urgency. "The window for planning is gone; the window for acting is open."
Example shape: "[Law name] is in effect. Enforcement is active. [What you need]. [Price]. Download today."

Both modes are offer-forward and integrity-preserving. Neither catastrophizes. Neither soft-pedals.

## What This Voice Does NOT Do

- **Does not explain what AI compliance is.** The buyer already knows they have a problem. Skip the orientation.
- **Does not use abstract noun piles.** "AI governance documentation templates for regulatory compliance frameworks" — no. "The 8 documents Illinois requires" — yes.
- **Does not use discovery-verbs.** No "discover," "explore," "unlock," "empower," "navigate." Those are newsletter-voice words. This voice is a counter, not a magazine.
- **Does not soften the legal fact.** "Could face potential penalties" is not this voice. "Up to $20,000 per violation" is. The statute says what it says.
- **Does not fabricate social proof.** No "trusted by X businesses," no "used by leading companies." None of it. Credibility comes from specificity: the law name, the section number, the primary source link.
- **Does not sell the process.** "Built from enacted statute text, not AI summaries" is a trust-signal that belongs on the product page's methodology section — not in the hero copy. The hero is for what you get, not how it was made.
- **Does not explain what "high-risk AI" means in the hero.** That's a blog paragraph. The hero says what you owe, not what the law defines.
- **Does not use passive constructions.** "Compliance documents are available for download" — no. "Get your Colorado documents" — yes.
- **Does not pitch consultation.** No "speak with an expert," no "schedule a demo," no "contact us for pricing." The product is instant-download at a fixed price. The voice reflects that.
- **Does not use the Realist/Credentialist journalist pattern** ("Illinois House Bill 3773 became law on January 1, 2026. If you manage hiring...") on marketing surfaces. That voice stays in the blog.

## Rationale: Why This Voice Fits the Buyer

The buyer who finds this site is not exploring AI compliance as a topic. They have a specific, named problem: a law applies to them, a date is real or already passed, and they need documentation. They are not in learning mode — they are in procurement mode. A procurement buyer needs to know: what do I get, does it cover my specific law, what does it cost, and how fast can I have it. Every sentence that doesn't answer one of those questions is noise.

The "compliance specialist at the counter" voice is the right register for procurement mode because it matches the buyer's internal state. They're not scared of directness — they want directness. The soft, explainer-mode voice signals "you might need to learn more before you're ready to buy." The specialist voice signals "I know exactly what you need — here it is." That's a conversion gap worth owning.

The two-mode structure (Deadline Approaching / Already Exposed) is essential because the buyer's emotional state differs based on whether the deadline is future or past. A Colorado buyer in April 2026 is anxious about an approaching date. An NYC employer who didn't know about Local Law 144 is in a different state — they're post-deadline, retrospectively exposed, and need a different kind of reassurance: not "you still have time," but "acting now is the right move; documentation is your first defense."
