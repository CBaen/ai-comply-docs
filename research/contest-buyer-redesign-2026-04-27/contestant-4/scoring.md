## Self-Reflection (Contestant 4)

Where my work is weakest relative to the field:

**The Build Order gap.** Contestant 2's Build Order (7 items, impact-per-hour sequenced, with a starred "ship this first" item) has no equivalent in my deliverables. I spec'd two mechanisms (flip logic, AlsoExposedStrip) with precision, but I never told the developer what to build in what order if continuity is limited. That is a real shippability gap — GL's continuity risk is documented in CLAUDE.md, and a contestant who understood that risk would have front-loaded the implementation sequencing.

**The recognition sentence.** Contestant 3's "recognition principle" — one statute-sourced insight per product page that demonstrates the site read the law, delivered without announcement — is an idea I didn't generate and should have. My two-mode frame (Deadline Approaching / Already Exposed) is mechanically stronger than any other entry's urgency system. But Contestant 3's recognition principle solves a different problem: why would a buyer trust the document quality? My entry trusts the StatusBadge to carry that weight. Contestant 3 built an additional trust signal that lives in the product page copy itself.

**The departure-board hero.** Contestant 2's right-column deadline sidebar (four law cards, status pills, clickable to product pages) is the most immediately legible urgency surface in the field. My right-column urgency panel achieves similar goals but is less defined at the component level — I spec'd the color (Enforcement Red / Deadline Amber) and the status flip logic but not the card-level anatomy in the same precision Contestant 2 did.

The two-mode frame and AlsoExposedStrip component spec remain unique contributions. The flip logic table (every UI element that changes on status flip, in one table, with copy variants for both modes across three product pages) is the most complete state-management spec in the field. But build sequence and recognition-principle are genuine gaps relative to peers.
