# Voice Spec — Marketing Surfaces

## The Voice I've Designed

**Name:** Transaction-First Declarative

This is the voice of a specialist who already knows you need what they have. No explanation of why compliance matters — you already know or you wouldn't be here. No warm-up, no preamble, no "in today's AI landscape." The sentence arrives with the statute in one hand and the document in the other. It states the obligation, names the product, gives the price, moves on. It sounds like the IRS crossed with a good hardware store: nothing personal, completely reliable, zero runaround.

## 5–10 Example Sentences

**Homepage hero:**
> Colorado. June 30. You're a deployer. Here are the 8 documents you owe.

**Homepage hero (alternative):**
> AI in your hiring. AI in your lending. AI in your customer decisions. Five states have laws about that. We built the documents.

**Product page intro — Colorado:**
> SB 24-205 requires a risk management program, impact assessments, consumer notices, and human oversight documentation. June 30, 2026 is when the AG starts counting. This package is those documents.

**Product page intro — Texas:**
> TRAIGA took effect January 1. No exemption for your size. No carveout for low-risk tools. One uncurable violation: up to $200,000. This package starts at $299.

**FAQ answer (what is this?):**
> Statute-sourced compliance documents. Not legal advice. Not a platform subscription. Documents your attorney can review and file. Instant download.

**Blog → product CTA:**
> The documents this law requires. $449. Instant download.

**Ad headline:**
> Colorado SB 24-205 Documents

**Trust signal:**
> Built from leg.colorado.gov. Not from a summary.

**Applicability confirmation:**
> If you deploy AI in Colorado, this applies to you. No revenue minimum. No size exemption.

**Penalty line:**
> Up to $20,000 per violation. Per consumer affected.

## What This Voice Does NOT Do

- **Does not explain what AI compliance is.** The buyer already knows they have a compliance problem. They came here to solve it.
- **Does not soften obligations.** "Could face fines" becomes "up to $20,000 per violation." The statute is not negotiable; neither is the copy.
- **Does not editorialize.** Not "you may want to consider" or "it's worth noting." States facts. Names obligations.
- **Does not use abstract nouns in headlines.** Not "AI governance," not "compliance solutions," not "regulatory frameworks." Uses law names (SB 24-205, HB 3773, Local Law 144) and product nouns (documents, templates, package).
- **Does not run long.** A homepage hero in this voice is 4–6 words per line. A product intro is 3 sentences. A CTA is 6 words or fewer.
- **Does not discovery-verb.** No "discover," "unlock," "explore," "transform," "harness." Every verb is transactional: get, download, comply, buy, file.
- **Does not fabricate social proof.** No "trusted by X businesses." No testimonials that don't exist. No "87% of businesses."
- **Does not use the em-dash as a personality crutch.** (The Realist/Credentialist voice uses em-dashes for precision. This voice uses short separate sentences instead.)
- **Does not say "built by AI."** Full stop.
- **Does not use "we" as the hero.** The buyer is the hero. "You owe these documents. We built them."

## Rationale Tied to Buyer Profile

The buyer the brief describes arrives under duress: counsel flagged an issue, a vendor form surfaced a gap, they read a news headline and searched in a panic. They are not browsing — they are triaging. Every word of explainer copy they have to read before finding the product is friction. The Transaction-First Declarative voice removes that friction by starting at the transaction. It assumes the buyer already understands the stakes (because they do — they just searched "Colorado AI law compliance") and skips to the offer. The statute-exact language (law names, section numbers, penalty figures) is not pedantry — it is proof. It proves the site knows what the law actually says, which is the only trust signal that matters to a buyer who is about to spend $299–$697 on compliance documents.

The voice is also designed to carry weight in AI Overview citations. AI Overviews prefer direct-answer content: "SB 24-205 requires X" rather than "SB 24-205 is a comprehensive law that regulates Y and Z and was passed in...". Short declarative sentences with statute-exact language get picked up by AI summarizers. Verbose explainers don't. The voice serves both the human buyer who is skimming and the LLM that is indexing.

## Voice Separation Table

| Surface | Voice | Pattern |
|---|---|---|
| Homepage hero | Transaction-First Declarative | Law name → deadline → obligation → product |
| Product page intro | Transaction-First Declarative | Obligation → deadline → what you get |
| Product page penalty section | Transaction-First Declarative | Statute-exact penalty → what triggers it → citation |
| Blog → product CTA | Transaction-First Declarative (compressed) | Law name → what you get → price |
| Ad headlines | Transaction-First Declarative (≤30 chars) | Law name or deadline → product noun |
| Blog post body | Realist/Credentialist (unchanged per style guide) | Keep existing voice |
| Blog post opening | Realist/Credentialist (unchanged) | Keep existing voice |
| FAQ on homepage | Transaction-First Declarative | Direct answer, no preamble |
| Footer | Neutral / institutional | No voice requirement |
