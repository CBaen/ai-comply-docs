# New Page Spec — Contestant 2 (Optional)

## Page: `/compliance-deadline-by-state`

**Route:** `https://aicompliancedocuments.com/compliance-deadline-by-state`

**Purpose:** A single-purpose landing page that answers the top pre-purchase buyer question — "which laws apply to my business, and when?" — and routes directly to product pages.

**Why this page is needed:**

Research confirmed that the #1 SMB objection before buying compliance documents is "does this even apply to me?" The current site routes this buyer to blog posts (which answer the question but don't convert) or to a generic `/products` index (which doesn't answer the question at all). This page intercepts applicability anxiety and converts it into a purchase decision by:

1. Showing all active deadlines in one place
2. Answering "who does each law cover" in 2 sentences per law
3. Linking directly to the product page for each law

It also targets the query "ai compliance deadlines by state 2026" — a Tier 1 query with high intent that nothing on the current site owns.

#### Card 1: New York City

**NYC Local Law 144 — Automated Employment Decision Tools**
**NYC Admin. Code § 20-870 et seq.**
**Status: IN EFFECT**
**Enforcement active:** July 5, 2023

Who it covers: Any employer or employment agency in NYC that uses an automated tool to screen or evaluate candidates for hiring or promotion. There is no employee-count minimum — a 5-person company using an AI screening tool is covered.

Penalty: $500 for the first violation. $500–$1,500 per subsequent violation per day. The DCWP has opened proactive investigations in 2026 — not just complaint-driven enforcement.

What you need: Annual bias audit, public audit summary, candidate notification, and website disclosure. 7 documents.

#### Card 2: Texas

**Texas TRAIGA — Responsible AI Governance Act (HB 149)**
**Tex. Gov't Code Ch. 552**
**Status: IN EFFECT**
**Effective:** January 1, 2026

Who it covers: Any business that develops or deploys AI systems in Texas, or affecting Texas consumers. No high-risk carveout, no small-business exemption. If you use AI in Texas operations, this law covers you.

Penalty: Curable violations: $10,000–$12,000. Uncurable violations: $80,000–$200,000. Continuing violations add $2,000–$40,000 per day on top of the base penalty. Attorney General enforces. 60-day cure period before action.

What you need: AI system inventory, risk management policy, consumer notification, bias evaluation, and disclosure documentation. 7 documents.

#### Card 3: Illinois

**Illinois HB3773 — AI in Employment Decisions**
**775 ILCS 5/2-102 (amended by Public Act 103-0804)**
**Status: IN EFFECT**
**Effective:** January 1, 2026

Who it covers: Any employer that uses AI in employment decisions and has employees or applicants in Illinois. Headquarters location doesn't matter. If your AI tools screen, rank, or influence decisions about people in Illinois, this law applies.

Penalty: Up to $16,000 for a first violation. Up to $42,500 if the employer has had one prior civil rights violation within five years. Up to $70,000 if the employer has two or more prior violations within seven years. (775 ILCS 5/8A-104)

What you need: Employee and applicant AI notice, AI system inventory, impact assessment framework, human oversight protocol, accommodation request form. Package includes 7 documents.

#### Card 4: Colorado

**Colorado SB 24-205 — Consumer Protections for Artificial Intelligence**
**C.R.S. § 6-1-1701 et seq.**
**Status: EFFECTIVE JUNE 30, 2026**
**Deadline:** June 30, 2026

Who it covers: Businesses that deploy AI systems making consequential decisions about Colorado consumers — in hiring, lending, insurance, housing, healthcare, or education. No revenue minimum, no employee minimum. If your AI system makes decisions that affect people's lives in these categories, you are a deployer.

Penalty: Up to $20,000 per violation under the Colorado Consumer Protection Act (C.R.S. § 6-1-112). Up to $50,000 per violation when the affected consumer is 60 or older (C.R.S. § 6-1-112(1)(c)). No private right of action — AG enforces.

What you need: Risk management policy, impact assessment, consumer notification, disclosure statement, algorithmic discrimination prevention plan, human oversight protocol, annual review tracker, compliance checklist. 8 documents.

#### Card 5: California

**California CCPA ADMT Regulations — Automated Decisionmaking Technology**
**Cal. Civ. Code § 1798.185 (CCPA ADMT provisions)**
**Status: PHASE 1 IN EFFECT**
**Phase 1 (risk assessments):** January 1, 2026
**Phase 2 (consumer notices/opt-out):** January 1, 2027

Who it covers: Businesses subject to CCPA that use automated systems to make significant decisions about California consumers. CCPA thresholds apply: $25M+ annual revenue, or processing 100,000+ consumers/devices annually, or deriving 50%+ revenue from data sales.

Penalty: $2,500 per violation; $7,500 per intentional violation (Cal. Civ. Code § 1798.155). CPPA and AG can both enforce.

What you need: Pre-use notice, opt-out mechanism documentation, ADMT impact assessment, consumer rights response procedures, data processing inventory, human oversight protocol, compliance checklist. 7 documents.
