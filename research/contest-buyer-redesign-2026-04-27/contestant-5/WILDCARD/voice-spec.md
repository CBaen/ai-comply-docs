# Voice Spec — Wildcard Synthesis

## The Voice: Calm Hardware Store

The person at the counter who knows exactly what you need and hands it to you. Short sentences. Specific nouns. No explanation of why you're here — you already know. No pitch. No warm-up. No "in today's regulatory environment." The sentence arrives with the statute in one hand and the download button in the other.

This is not the Pragmatic Realist voice from the blog style guide. That voice is an educator explaining what a law means. This voice is a specialist confirming what you owe and delivering it. Both are correct — on different surfaces.

---

## The Two Urgency Modes (from C4 — attributed)

The voice has one name but two registers. Apply by law status.

**Mode 1 — Deadline Approaching** (Colorado SB 24-205, any law with a future effective date):
The clock is visible. The window is real. The voice names the date without catastrophizing.
> "Colorado SB 24-205. June 30, 2026. 8 documents. $449. Here."

**Mode 2 — Already Exposed** (Illinois HB3773, NYC Local Law 144, Texas TRAIGA):
The deadline has passed. The exposure is current. The voice is calm, not panicked — "acting now is the right move."
> "Illinois HB3773 is in effect. You owe these documents now. $397. Instant download."

Both modes are offer-forward. Neither catastrophizes. Neither soft-pedals. The distinction is temporal: one voice says "you have a window," the other says "the window is gone, but acting now is your defense."

## What This Voice Does NOT Do

- Does not explain what AI compliance is. The buyer knows. Skip the orientation.
- Does not open with empathy preamble ("If you're like most small business owners...").
- Does not soften penalty language. "Up to $20,000 per violation" — not "could face potential fines."
- Does not use passive voice. "The law requires" not "requirements are placed."
- Does not use discovery verbs: no "discover," "explore," "unlock," "navigate," "transform."
- Does not fabricate social proof: no "trusted by X businesses," no testimonials that don't exist.
- Does not explain the product methodology in the hero. Methodology belongs in the trust section.
- Does not use the Realist/Credentialist pattern on marketing surfaces. That voice stays in the blog.
- Does not say "built by AI." Full stop.
- Does not run long. A hero is 2-3 sentences. A CTA is 6 words or fewer.
- Does not use abstract noun piles. Not "comprehensive AI governance documentation solutions." "The 8 documents the law requires."

## The Recognition Principle (from C3 — attributed)

One sentence per product page that surfaces something the buyer didn't know to ask. Statute-sourced. Delivered without explanation. In the marketing voice, not the blog voice.

The sentence demonstrates the site read the statute — not just the summary. The buyer who needs it will stop. The buyer who doesn't will scroll.

**Requirements for a valid recognition sentence:**
1. It must be statute-sourced (cite the specific section)
2. It must surface something the buyer didn't know to ask — not a restatement of the applicability checklist
3. It must be one sentence in the marketing voice (no following explanation)
4. It must appear in the applicability section, after the main qualification bullets
5. It must be verifiable against a .gov primary source before publishing

**Examples (ready to paste):**
- Colorado: "If you also built or substantially modified the AI tool you're deploying, the statute assigns you separate developer obligations on top of these — including disclosure requirements to your own deployers." (C.R.S. § 6-1-1702)
- Illinois: "If you use zip codes as a proxy for location in any AI employment decision, that's explicitly prohibited by name in the statute — not inferred from anti-discrimination principles." (775 ILCS 5/2-102(L))
- NYC: "The bias audit must be conducted by an independent auditor — your HR team cannot conduct it internally. These documents are the pre-audit infrastructure, not the audit itself." (NYC Admin. Code § 20-871)
- Texas: "If your AI tool makes a consequential decision about a Texas consumer without meaningful human oversight, the 60-day cure period begins only after the AG sends notice — not after you discover the violation." (HB 149, TRAIGA § 552.053) [REQUIRES PRIMARY SOURCE VERIFICATION before publish]

## Rationale

The buyer arrives under duress. Counsel flagged an issue, a vendor form surfaced a gap, or they searched in a panic at 11pm. They are not browsing — they are in procurement mode. A procurement buyer needs: what do I get, does it cover my specific law, what does it cost, how fast can I have it. Every sentence that doesn't answer one of those questions is friction.

The Calm Hardware Store voice removes that friction because it matches the buyer's internal state: they want to confirm they're in the right place, then transact. It earns credibility through specificity (law name, section number, penalty figure, deadline date) not through credential-stacking. The recognition sentence earns the deeper credibility: it proves the site read the statute, not a summary. That's a trust signal no competitor can buy.
