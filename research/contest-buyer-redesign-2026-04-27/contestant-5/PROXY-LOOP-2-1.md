## The coaching push

The EEOC post is the right anchor. 1,833 impressions at position 4.16 with a 0.11% CTR when the expected CTR at position 4 is approximately 7.2% is a gap that, if closed even partially, is worth more than any other single change on the site. Your title rewrite proposal ("EEOC Removed AI Hiring Guidance. Illinois, NYC, and Colorado Filled the Gap.") is buyer-intent and news-shaped. That's the right direction.

Now look at what's underneath the title change. Your spec includes a new closing CTA block for the EEOC post:
"Illinois HB3773 is in effect. NYC Local Law 144 is in effect. Colorado SB 24-205 takes effect June 30, 2026. The documents these laws require are here."

That's good. Short. Specific. Offer-forward. And then three law-specific CTAs + a "find your state" link.

Here is the gap: what happens to a reader who clicks the EEOC post from that position-4.16 SERP entry and reads the first 200 words? Right now, the post doesn't have a mid-article CTA. Your spec adds one: "mid-article bridge (after the EEOC section, before the state law section)." That bridge is the part most likely to catch the buyer who arrived with intent but hasn't reached the bottom of the post.

Your spec doesn't give the mid-article bridge the same copy specificity as the closing CTA. You said "insert a bridge." What does the bridge say? What is the exact line that interrupts the reader who just learned what EEOC removed and tells them where to go now?

One candidate: "Here's what replaced it." Then a product CTA. The reader who just processed "EEOC removed AI hiring guidance" is in the exact state of mind to receive "here's what filled the gap" — that's the EEOC post's entire thesis applied as a conversion mechanism.

## The one alignment question

Your priority list ranks cost-for-small-business 4th despite it having the lowest impression count (479). Your reasoning is presumably that it's the closest to purchase intent — a buyer reading about cost has already decided they need to buy, they're just calculating whether the price is right.

That reasoning is sound. But 479 impressions means fewer buyers are finding it. The question isn't whether to rewrite it — it's whether the title needs work before the CTA does. If the cost post is at the right position but with a low-intent title, more buyers might be landing on it without recognizing it as relevant to their specific situation.

What is the current title of the cost-for-small-business post? Does it name a specific law, a specific penalty amount, a specific buyer type? Or is it a generic "how much does AI compliance cost" frame?

If it's generic, the rewrite priority should be title/meta first (same as the EEOC post), not CTA first. A buyer searching "Colorado AI compliance cost small business" needs to see their state and their situation in the title before they click.

You've built the right blog strategy. Now check whether each post's title is the thing that needs fixing first — before the closing CTA.
