# Proxy Field Observation — Loop 1

## What the Field Converged On (and What That Means)

**Four of five contestants proposed a `/compliance-deadline-by-state` or functionally equivalent page.** Contestants 2, 3, 4, and 5 all proposed this concept, with slightly different names (`/compliance-deadline-by-state`, `/compliance-deadline-tracker`, `/deadline-checker`). This is strong signal — the field independently identified the same gap: buyers who know they have a problem but can't yet navigate the product catalog. The convergence here is not groupthink; it's five independent analyses reaching the same conclusion. Worth noting for the field summary, but you probably don't need to tell the contestants what the others proposed — the convergence just validates the concept.

**Every contestant proposed making the H1 visible** (currently `sr-only`). This is the field's unanimous structural fix. Agreed — it's the right call.

**Every contestant rewrote "Browse Products" to buyer-task language.** "Find My Compliance Documents," "Get My Compliance Documents," "Find Your State's Documents" — all variations on the same fix. Unanimous, correct, worth flagging as the single easiest and highest-probability win to ship first.

---

## The One Real Divergence (Contestants 1 and 3 — both can't be fully right)

Contestant 1 says: pivot AWAY from informational queries, because AI Overviews consume them. Targeting transactional queries is the structural response.

Contestant 3 says: optimize FOR AI Overview citation, because brands cited inside AI Overviews get 35% more organic clicks. (Actually that insight is from Contestant 2, but Contestant 3's `/compliance-deadline-tracker` page is also structured to earn AI Overview citations.)

These are not logically incompatible — you could pursue transactional query targeting AND optimize for AI Overview citation on the informational content that already ranks. But they rest on different bets about where the leverage is. Contestant 1 is betting that the informational query traffic is structurally unreachable and the effort should go to a different audience. Contestant 2 is betting that the existing informational traffic can be partially recovered if the site earns citation inside the AI Overview box. This is the only genuine strategic divergence in Round 1, and it's worth presenting it as a real question in the field summary: "One approach says abandon informational queries; another says earn your way into the AI Overview that dominates them. Here's what each requires and what each risks."

## Voice Quality Across the Field

All five contestants hit the Realist/Credentialist pattern in at least some of their deliverables. The range is:

- Contestants 1 and 2: Consistently hold the voice through all deliverables, including product page and blog CTA copy. The penalty math ("50 applicants × $70,000 = ...") is the Credentialist doing its job.
- Contestants 3 and 5: Strong in homepage copy, slightly weaker in product page template — a few passages slip toward descriptive rather than declarative. Nothing egregious.
- Contestant 4: The strongest structural insight (affirmative defense as conversion bridge) but one passage in the penalty section risks tipping toward alarm copy rather than statute-statement copy: "The AG is not required to wait for rulemaking before bringing an action" is a claim that requires primary-source verification before it goes live. Flag for the build instance.

## Research Integrity Across the Field

Three contestants (1, 2, 5) explicitly flagged where they couldn't reach primary sources — Texas TRAIGA bill text (PDF binary or 404), Colorado penalty ($20K via secondary sources, not directly from statute text). That's the correct behavior. Two contestants (3, 4) cited secondary sources for Colorado and Texas penalties without flagging the secondary nature. This matters because Colorado's $20K penalty traces through CCPA enforcement, not directly from SB24-205 text. All five used that number — it is likely correct, but whoever builds this should verify C.R.S. § 6-1-112 directly before it goes live in any buyer-facing copy.

## Which Contestant Needs the Most Push

Contestant 3 went the most conservative. The surgical restraint is a real virtue and may be the right strategic call — but as I noted in their loop note, there's a risk that the restraint is a default rather than a considered choice. If their Loop 2 work still can't articulate "the move I chose not to make," that's a signal that the work was conserved rather than decided. Watch this in Round 2.

Contestant 4 has the most distinctive structural contribution (affirmative defense as conversion bridge, urgency bar with penalty amounts, page reorder), but the deadline-only frame needs to address already-in-force laws explicitly. If they can add a sibling frame for NYC/Illinois/Texas, their strategy becomes the most comprehensive of the field.

## What Loop 2 Perspectives Should Probe

1. After seeing the field summary, which contestants have the most to gain from committing harder to their unique angle vs. converging toward the consensus?
2. Contestant 2's AI Overview citation strategy is the most distinctive in the field. They should be pushed to be more concrete about what "earning AI Overview citation" actually requires in practice — what content format, what schema, what authority signals.
3. Contestant 1 should be pushed to reconcile the pivot thesis with the EEOC post specifically — is it abandoned, or is there a path for it?
4. Contestant 5's "affirmative defense" insight from their product page is the strongest single conversion line in the field. They should be pushed to use it in their homepage and blog CTA pattern, not just the product page.
