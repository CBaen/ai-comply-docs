## Voice Register for Ads

Maximum compression of the Calm Authority voice. Name the law, name the document type, name the price if it fits, give one imperative. No soft verbs. No feature lists. The buyer is in a search results page or a social feed — they have two seconds. Every ad acts like a hardware store shelf label, not a pitch.

**What these ads are NOT:** They are not brand-awareness ads. They are not educational. They do not explain what AI compliance is. They assume the buyer already knows they have a problem and are shopping for the solution.

## Reddit Native Ad

**Placement:** r/smallbusiness, r/humanresources, r/Compliance
**Format:** Native text post with image — blends with organic post style, not a display banner

**Title (≤60 chars):**
> "Illinois AI hiring law is live. Here's what you actually need."

**Body (≤500 chars):**
> If you use any AI tool in your hiring process and you have employees in Illinois — HB3773 is already in effect.
>
> The law requires: employee notices, an AI system inventory, and an impact assessment showing your tools aren't discriminating by protected class.
>
> Penalties are up to $70,000 per aggrieved person. Not per complaint — per person affected.
>
> We built the 7-document compliance package from the actual enacted statute. $449, instant download.
>
> aicompliancedocuments.com/products/illinois-hb3773

**Image direction:**
Unsplash query: `"HR manager hiring office computer"` — a real person at a real desk, looking at a screen. Not staged. Natural light. No AI visualizations, no courtroom imagery. The image should look like the buyer — an HR manager at a mid-size business.

**Native-feel rationale:** The title is framed as an informational post ("here's what you actually need") rather than an ad headline ("buy now"). This reduces scroll-past on Reddit, where users are hostile to obvious ads. The body delivers real information first (the law requires X, Y, Z) before the commercial offer. The statutory citation in the body signals credibility — it reads like someone who's done the research and is sharing it, not selling.

## Retargeting Concept

**Target:** Visitor who viewed `/products/colorado-sb24-205` or `/products/illinois-hb3773` and left without purchasing.

**Platform:** Google Display / Meta Audience Network

**Headline:** `June 30 hasn't moved.`

**Description:** `You looked at the Colorado compliance package. The deadline didn't change. Your documents are still there.`

**CTA:** `Complete My Order`

**Image direction:** Unsplash query: `"calendar desk office deadline"` — a simple desk calendar, no stock-photo staging. The image should communicate "time is passing" without alarm-imagery.

**Rationale:** The headline uses the same deadlines-don't-move logic as the homepage hero, but in first-person context: "you looked, you didn't act, the deadline is the same." The description avoids pressure ("you're running out of time!") and instead states a fact ("the deadline didn't change") plus a warm recall ("your documents are still there"). The CTA is outcome-framed: "Complete My Order" implies they've already started a process, which they have — they visited the product page.

**Retargeting window:** 14 days post-visit. After 14 days, switch to a different creative with updated deadline proximity copy.

## Imagery Direction Across All Ads

The visual register for ad creative is **working professional in motion** — someone doing their job, not posing for a stock photo. The compliance problem interrupts their actual workday; the imagery should reflect that reality.

Avoid: handshakes, scales of justice, AI neural networks, pure-white offices, executive headshots, abstract legal symbols.

Use: real desk environments, screens with readable content, people in mid-task rather than posed-for-camera, natural or office ambient light, geographic specificity where possible (Colorado mountains visible through an office window signals "Colorado business" more efficiently than any copy).

One image per ad, not a carousel. Static images outperform carousels for compliance/legal intent queries — the buyer is not browsing, they're evaluating a single option.
