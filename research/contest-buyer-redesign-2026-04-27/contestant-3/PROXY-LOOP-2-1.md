# Proxy Coach — Loop 2, Contestant 3

## Your assigned perspective

The hardware store model is your best metaphor. "You came in for a fitting, here it is." The person behind the counter at a good hardware store doesn't wait to be asked — they see what you're holding and they say "you need a half-inch compression fitting for that, not a quarter-inch, and they're on aisle 6." They demonstrate mastery before they sell you anything.

Where in your design does the site visibly demonstrate that mastery without claiming consultation?

---

## The coaching push

Your visual direction is the field's tightest semantic color system. Document Blue for CTAs only. Deadline Amber strictly for urgency signals. Statute Green strictly for verified badges. No color drift. That discipline is real expertise made visible through restraint — it's exactly the kind of mastery the hardware store person has, expressed in a design decision.

But the buyer can't see the color rule system. They see the result.

Here is what the buyer sees: the right-side product selector in the hero. Three rows. Law shortname, status badge, price, arrow. Clean. No animation. No carousel. A buyer who arrived for Illinois HB3773 scans the list, finds their law, clicks. Done.

That is the hardware store working. The fitting is on the shelf. The buyer doesn't have to ask.

Now look at what's missing. The hardware store person doesn't just point to aisle 6 — they also say "and if you're replacing that valve, you'll also need the pipe tape, it's right next to it." That's not upselling. That's mastery recognizing a pattern the buyer doesn't know to ask about.

In your design, where is the moment where the site recognizes something the buyer didn't ask about?

The product page is where this lives — or should. Your product-page template spec preserves the document list and the affirmative defense section. Both are good. But the affirmative defense section is under Section 2 ("The Penalty for Non-Compliance"), which means it's framed as the reason to buy. It's a conversion mechanism. That's correct.

What it doesn't do yet is demonstrate *recognition*. The hardware store moment would be: after the buyer lands on the Colorado page, the site immediately acknowledges that they're a *deployer* (not a developer), and the document set reflects that. Or: the page surfaces the specific distinction between the deployer affirmative defense and the developer disclosure requirements — without being asked — because the site knows that a buyer landing on this page is usually a deployer who doesn't know they have developer-side obligations too if they're also building AI tools.

---

## The concrete redesign question

Your "Section 1: Does This Apply to You?" opener is three declarative lines:
"You use AI in employment, housing, credit, healthcare, insurance, or education decisions. You serve Colorado consumers. This law applies to you."

That is the hardware store telling you which aisle. It's correct and fast.

But the hardware store person who knows the stock would also say: "If you're the one deploying the tool, not the one who built it, the compliance obligation lands differently." The Colorado law distinguishes deployers and developers. Your document list covers the deployer side.

What is one sentence — one — in Section 1 that demonstrates the site knows the difference between a deployer and a developer without turning it into an explainer? The buyer who reads that sentence knows immediately that the site is not a generic compliance template factory. It's a site built by someone who read the statute.

That sentence is the guy in the apron pointing to the right fitting. Find it.
