# Voice Spec — Marketing Surfaces

## The Voice in One Paragraph

This is the voice of a business that knows exactly what it has, who needs it, and why the answer is not complicated. It does not explain the law — the blog does that. It says: here is what the law requires, here are the documents that satisfy it, here is the price, here is the download button. The register is the same calm confidence a hardware store uses when a customer walks in with a broken pipe: they don't lecture you on plumbing — they hand you the fitting. Short sentences. No qualifiers. Product before process. Outcome before methodology. The buyer's situation is named before the solution is offered, but only barely — because the buyer who arrived here already knows their situation. They need the next step, not another explanation.

---

## Five to Ten Example Sentences in the New Voice

**Homepage hero:**
> "Your state passed an AI law. Here are the documents."

**Product page opening:**
> "Colorado requires 8 specific documents before June 30. These are them."

**Product card copy:**
> "Illinois HB3773. 5 documents. $397. In effect now."

**FAQ answer (short form):**
> "Yes, it applies to you. If you use AI in hiring in Illinois, this is your law."

**Ad headline:**
> "Colorado AI Law. Documents. $449."

**Blog-to-product CTA:**
> "The document this section is describing costs $449 and downloads in 5 minutes."

**Sidebar purchase card:**
> "64 days. 8 documents. $449. Done."

**Trust signal:**
> "Built from the enacted statute. Not a summary. The law itself."

**Penalty framing:**
> "Up to $20,000 per violation. The documents that prevent that: $449."

**Navigation:**
> "Find Your Documents."

## What This Voice Does NOT Do

- Does NOT explain the law before naming the product. (Blog explains. Product page sells.)
- Does NOT use multi-clause sentences with subordinating conjunctions when a period would do.
- Does NOT use "discover," "unlock," "transform," "empower," "leverage," or "navigate."
- Does NOT soften penalty language with "could face" or "may be subject to." Statute-exact amounts, no hedging.
- Does NOT say "trusted by X businesses" or any claim without a verified primary source.
- Does NOT say "built by AI" or imply the content is AI-generated.
- Does NOT give process before outcome. ("We read the statute and then we built the templates and then we verified the citations and then..." — wrong. Outcome first.)
- Does NOT answer a question that wasn't asked. If the buyer is on the Colorado product page, they already know Colorado has an AI law. Skip the orientation paragraph.
- Does NOT pad with transition phrases: "In today's rapidly evolving landscape," "As we enter 2026," "That's why we built..." — cut to the sentence that matters.
- Does NOT mistake brevity for coldness. The voice is confident and useful, not abrupt.

## Rationale Tied to Buyer Profile

The buyer arrives with one of three problems: a lawyer flagged a gap, a vendor contract surfaced a requirement, or they Googled their state's AI law and got scared. In all three cases, they have already done enough reading to know they have a problem. They do not need more explanation. What they need is evidence that this site has the specific thing their law requires — and then the smallest possible distance between that evidence and the purchase button.

The Pragmatic Realist + Precise Credentialist voice in the blog is correct for a buyer who is still in the learning phase. But a buyer on the product page is past that phase. Subjecting them to another paragraph of "here's how the law works" is friction, not helpfulness. The marketing voice removes that friction by trusting that the buyer knows what they came for, and getting out of their way.

Short sentences also improve SERP snippet quality. A 12-word sentence that says exactly what the product is outperforms a 40-word sentence in AI Overview citation, voice search, and title-tag display. The voice is designed for buyer conversion AND discoverability — they are the same thing when the copy is tight enough.

---

## The Hardware Store Test

Apply this test to any proposed marketing copy: "Would a hardware store say it?" A hardware store does not write "our team of experts curates a comprehensive selection of plumbing solutions designed to address the full spectrum of residential and commercial needs." It says "Plumbing. Aisle 7." The marketing voice for this site is one step above that — it names the buyer's situation, names the product, names the price, and stops.
