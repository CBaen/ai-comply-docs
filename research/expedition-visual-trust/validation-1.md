## Divergence-First Protocol

### 1. Evidence Challenges — Claims That Don't Hold Up Under Scrutiny

---

#### Challenge 1.1 — Cornell University study is misapplied (Team 1, Finding 1)

**Claim:** "A Cornell University study (2019) found customers are more likely to trust unique images showing actual products or services versus stock images." (Team 1, via GroupM7)

**What I found:** The Cornell paper (arxiv.org/abs/1811.10648) is real. Its findings do support the direction claimed. However, the research context is peer-to-peer secondhand marketplaces (think Craigslist or eBay for used goods — approximately 75,000 listings). The study examined whether high-quality photos of the actual second-hand item outperform stock photos in building trust for individual P2P sellers.

**The problem:** This is not a B2B compliance software study, not a digital product study, and not a professional services study. Applying a P2P marketplace finding ("show the actual used bike, not a manufacturer stock photo") to justify removing lifestyle imagery from a compliance template site is a category transfer with no validated bridge. The direction of the finding is plausible — authentic beats generic — but the evidentiary weight Team 1 assigned it ("Battle-Tested, multiple converging sources") is not justified by this specific citation.

**Verdict:** The conclusion (stock imagery is less trusted than authentic visuals) is supported by the research direction, but the Cornell citation is misattributed to B2B trust contexts. The finding itself still points in the right direction — it just cannot carry the weight Team 1 assigned it as a "multiple converging sources" anchor.

---

#### Challenge 1.2 — The "65% AI fingerprint" statistic is fabricated (Team 3, Section 1.1)

**Claim:** "The combination of Next.js + Tailwind CSS + Shadcn UI + Lucide Icons + Radix UI is the single strongest signal that a site was generated by an AI coding tool... This combination appears in 65%+ of AI-generated sites." Cited to aiwebsitedetector.com.

**What I found:** I fetched aiwebsitedetector.com directly. The site does not cite a 65% figure for Next.js + Tailwind + Shadcn UI as an AI fingerprint anywhere in its documentation or methodology. The only "65%" on the site appears as a detection confidence score for a specific scan result (yahoo.com), not as a population statistic about AI-generated sites. The site also states accuracy is "85–99%" for major platform detection but provides zero peer review or published research behind these numbers. It is a commercial tool, not an academic source.

**The problem:** Team 3 invented a specific statistic from a source that does not contain it. The underlying claim — that Next.js + Tailwind is a common AI coding tool stack — is plausible and observed. But the 65% precision is fabricated.

**Verdict:** Flag and remove. The 65% figure is not in the cited source. The broader point about stack fingerprinting being a signal is reasonable as an observation, but should be stated as observational inference, not a sourced statistic.

---

#### Challenge 1.3 — "38% of web visitors leave due to generic design" is misattributed (Team 3, Section 1.4)

**Claim:** "Generic design drives 38% of web visitors away (Neil Patel, 2025 — cited by 925 Studios)." Used to argue compliance buyers are more susceptible than average.

**What I found:** I fetched the 925 Studios source directly. The article does not attribute this statistic to Neil Patel. It attributes it to Figma (2026): "Poor design and content drive 38% of web visitors away (Figma, 2026)." Neil Patel is cited separately in the same article for a different claim about AI vs. human content performance. I then searched for the Figma 2026 source and could not locate a Figma report containing this specific "38%" figure — Figma's 2026 web design statistics page returned related but different data points.

**The problem:** Team 3 misread the attribution in the source article, then created a false citation chain. The statistic may exist somewhere, but neither the attribution (Neil Patel) nor the source (2025) matches what the cited article actually says, and I cannot verify the underlying Figma claim either.

**Verdict:** Double misattribution. The stat as cited is unreliable. It should be dropped or replaced with a directly verifiable source.

---

#### Challenge 1.4 — "97% of buyers check your website first" is unsourced (Team 1, Finding 3; Team 3, Section 1.4)

**Claim:** Both Team 1 and Team 3 cite "97% of B2B buyers check your website first." Both trace it to Vendict.

**What I found:** I fetched the Vendict source. The 97% figure appears in the article but with no source citation attached to it. Vendict does not attribute it to Gartner, Forrester, or any primary research body. It appears to be an editorial assertion. Independent searching found a DesignRush article stating "90% of B2B buyers compare websites" and the Sopro B2B stats roundup, but neither surfaces the specific 97% figure from a primary source.

**Verdict:** A widely-circulated but unverifiable figure. It may be a distorted version of a real stat from a primary study, but it cannot be cited as fact. Both teams relied on it without checking whether Vendict had a source. The directional claim (buyers check vendor websites extensively) is well-supported by other research. The 97% precision is not.

#### Challenge 1.5 — Gartner's rep-free preference stat is already outdated (Team 1, Finding 2)

**Claim:** "Gartner (2025) found 61% of B2B buyers prefer a rep-free buying experience." Cited to a June 2025 Gartner press release.

**What I found:** Gartner published a newer version of this research in March 2026 showing the figure has risen to 67%. The June 2025 study (61%) is real and correctly cited, but there is a more current Gartner publication that supersedes it: "Gartner Sales Survey Finds 67% of B2B Buyers Prefer a Rep-Free Experience" (published 2026-03-09).

**Verdict:** Not a fabrication — the 61% figure is accurate for its publication date. But as of this expedition's date (2026-03-25), it is stale by 16 days. The correct current figure is 67%. Team 1 should have used the current Gartner release. Both figures support the same conclusion; the point stands. Worth correcting for publication.

---

#### Challenge 1.6 — TrustRadius free trial stat is applied outside its scope (Team 1, Priority 3)

**Claim:** "TrustRadius research found free trials are the single most influential resource for 74% of buyers who used them." Used to argue document samples are the "closest equivalent."

**What I found:** The TrustRadius 74% figure is verifiable and real (2023 B2B Buying Disconnect report). However, the statistic specifically applies to tech software buyers evaluating SaaS products, not document template buyers. More precisely: 74% of those who used a free trial said it was the most influential resource — but only 40% of B2B buyers used a free trial at all. The stat is conditional on trial usage, not universal.

**Verdict:** The statistic is real but the application is a stretch. "Document sample as free trial equivalent" is a reasonable analogy, but Team 1 should have flagged this as an extrapolation rather than presenting it as evidence. The direction of the recommendation is sound.

---

#### Challenge 1.7 — Forrester's "90% trust peers over vendor content" figure is imprecise (Team 1, Finding 4)

**Claim:** "Forrester (2023): over 90% of respondents trust peers in their industry more than vendor content."

**What I found:** Searching Forrester's published research returns a close but not identical finding: "over 90% of survey respondents say that buyers completely or somewhat trust peers in their industry" — but the comparison is nuanced. Salespeople from vendors are least trusted (29%), while peers are trusted at over 90%. This supports the directional claim but the framing ("over vendor content specifically") is a slight overstatement. Vendor content itself was not rated at 10% — the contrast is against active salespeople, not written content.

**Verdict:** The directional finding is real. The precise framing is imprecise. Minor issue, but in a compliance context where precision matters to the buyer audience, it is worth noting.

### 2. Contradictions — Where Teams Disagree

---

#### Contradiction 2.1 — Stock photo severity: "trust destroyer" vs. "medium priority"

**Team 1** frames stock lifestyle photography as a primary trust-damaging signal warranting Priority 1 action: "The research is clear enough to act on without A/B testing."

**Team 3** ranks stock photography as **medium priority** for this specific audience: "Stock photography — registers as generic but not fraudulent." Team 3 explicitly places it below the absence of human identity and below social proof gaps, noting "designers notice, compliance buyers less so."

**Assessment:** These are not irreconcilable — they reflect different framings of the same evidence. Team 1 is reporting what the research says about stock photos in general. Team 3 is applying that finding to the specific buyer and concluding it is not the first thing to fix. Team 3's framing is more audience-calibrated for this specific product. The contradiction is a priority disagreement, not an evidence disagreement. Team 3 wins the argument on audience-specificity. Removing stock photos matters, but it is not the highest-leverage fix.

---

#### Contradiction 2.2 — "No human identity" as primary vs. secondary concern

**Team 3** argues the absence of any named founder or attributed person is "the primary AI tell" and "the primary trust blocker" — the single highest-leverage fix. This is the centerpiece of their report.

**Team 1** does not address founder identity or human attribution at all. The gap table mentions "Author/expert attribution" at Medium impact/Low difficulty but does not elevate it to Priority 1 or 2.

**Team 2** notes the About page has "no identifiable credentials, advisors, or external validation" as a gap, but treats it as one of five gaps, not the primary concern.

**Assessment:** This is a genuine analytical gap in Teams 1 and 2. Team 3 is correct that in the compliance domain, buyer due diligence includes accountability questions. The research brief asked specifically about what makes a compliance site trustworthy for $149–$997 purchases — and Team 3 is the only team that engaged with the accountability dimension of that question seriously. Teams 1 and 2 underweighted it. Team 3's elevation of this issue is the most important unique contribution of the expedition.

---

#### Contradiction 2.3 — Blog content: not addressed by Teams 1 and 2

**Team 3** identifies the "all 20 blog posts in one month" pattern as a significant AI-detection signal and labels it "the exact pattern of an AI content push."

**Teams 1 and 2** do not address the blog publication pattern at all.

**Assessment:** Not a direct contradiction, but a significant coverage gap. Team 3 is raising a real signal that the other teams missed entirely. Whether 20 posts in 12 days is a trust problem or an SEO strategy question is debatable, but the signal it sends to skeptical compliance buyers evaluating organizational legitimacy is worth including in the design direction.

### 3. Alignment Drift — Where Findings Drift From the Research Brief

---

#### Drift 3.1 — The research brief asked about stock photos specifically; teams generalized correctly but Team 3 overextended

The brief was precise: "We added stock images assuming they humanize the site. Do stock photos build or undermine trust?"

Teams 1 and 2 answered this question and provided design direction. Good.

Team 3 answered a larger question: "What makes this site look AI-generated?" That broader question is useful and adjacent, but several of Team 3's tells (em dash density, numbered circle pattern, color palette specificity) drift from what a compliance officer evaluating a $449 purchase actually cares about. Team 3 acknowledged this in their Priority Assessment section (Section 5), which partially corrects the drift. But the checklist of 10 tells risks diluting the most important findings with design-critique observations that do not affect purchase decisions.

**Verdict:** Team 3 produced valuable adjacent research but should weight findings explicitly for the buyer profile stated in the brief. Their Section 5 does this but it comes late and competes with the longer list.

---

#### Drift 3.2 — Team 2 evaluated site design patterns without testing the hypothesis directly

The brief asked whether stock photos build or undermine trust. Team 2 answered: "no successful competitor uses lifestyle stock photography as a primary trust mechanism." This is an observation about what competitors do, not direct evidence about what trust effects stock photos have.

Team 2's competitor analysis is the strongest structural evidence in the expedition — eight live sites, all showing the same pattern — but it is correlation, not causation. These sites may have removed stock photos for many reasons, or may never have used them. That zero of eight competitors use stock lifestyle photography is powerful converging evidence, but Team 2 presents it as if it answers the trust question when it actually shows what the design consensus has become.

**Verdict:** Team 2's evidence is strong and directionally correct. The analytical framing slightly overstates what competitor observation proves. Worth noting but does not undermine the conclusion.

### 4. Missing Angles — What Wasn't Researched

---

#### Gap 4.1 — No direct A/B test evidence for B2B professional services specifically

All three teams note at various points that the evidence against stock photos comes from adjacent contexts: P2P marketplaces, B2C e-commerce, general B2B SaaS. No team surfaced published A/B test data specifically for legal or compliance template sites comparing stock photos versus documentary imagery versus no imagery. This gap is acknowledged in Team 1's confidence table ("Hero images at low opacity create trust ambiguity — Medium — Logical inference") but the gap is larger than one line suggests.

The expedition's core recommendation — remove or replace stock lifestyle photos — is well-supported by converging adjacent evidence. But it is still an inference applied to a specific context without direct experimental proof.

---

#### Gap 4.2 — No price-point-specific trust research

The brief specifies $149–$997 purchase decisions. This price range is significant: it sits above impulse purchases but below enterprise procurement thresholds. None of the three teams found or cited research that specifically examines trust signals at this price band for digital products. Trust signal research for B2B SaaS typically addresses enterprise (five-figure contracts) or consumer (sub-$50). The middle band — individual professional spending several hundred dollars on a digital document — is a different trust equation that was not researched directly.

---

#### Gap 4.3 — No research on compliance officer due diligence behavior specifically

Team 3 makes strong claims about how compliance officers evaluate vendor accountability ("they need to know who is accountable if this is wrong"). This is plausible professional inference, but no team fetched or cited published research on compliance officer purchase behavior specifically. Vendict and Forrester data covers general B2B buyers. The specific claim that compliance professionals apply heightened scrutiny to vendor identity and credentialing — compared to, say, a marketing manager buying a SaaS tool — is stated as fact but is supported only by professional inference, not cited research.

---

#### Gap 4.4 — No testing of the site's existing documentary imagery

Team 1 recommends replacing lifestyle stock photos with documentary imagery (screenshots of statute text, state seals, regulatory documents). This recommendation is sensible but untested. None of the teams verified whether the site already has this kind of imagery in any form, or analyzed whether the existing hero background imagery (e.g., the Denver skyline on the Colorado page) is actually performing the geographic-documentary function Team 1 describes. Team 2 noted "the Colorado Denver skyline at 30% opacity" exists but did not evaluate whether it is working as intended.

---

#### Gap 4.5 — Team 2's competitor set has a notable bias

Team 2 examined 8 sites but noted that 3 of the most relevant comparators (Drata, TermsFeed, Thomson Reuters Practical Law) were blocked. The sites that were accessible are not all direct analogues: Vanta is enterprise SaaS, IAPP is a professional association, Nolo is a legacy brand with 50 years of trust equity. The most relevant peer — a small, new, digital-first compliance document site with no brand equity — was not represented in the set. The competitor analysis is valuable but describes what established players do, not what works for a new entrant in the same tier.

### 5. Agreements — Where Teams Converge (High Confidence)

The following conclusions are reached independently by multiple teams using different research approaches. These are the highest-confidence findings from the expedition.

---

#### Agreement 5.1 — Stock lifestyle photography serves no trust function for this buyer (HIGH CONFIDENCE)

All three teams conclude independently that the current stock lifestyle images (professionals typing, documents on desks, team meetings) do not build trust with professional compliance buyers. Team 1 cites psychological and marketing research. Team 2 finds zero of eight competitors using lifestyle stock photography for trust-building. Team 3 classifies it as an AI-detection signal. Three different research angles, same conclusion.

The precise mechanism differs (trust penalty vs. competitive disadvantage vs. AI-tell), but the operational direction is identical: these images should be removed or replaced with domain-specific visuals.

---

#### Agreement 5.2 — Third-party review platform absence is the largest addressable trust gap (HIGH CONFIDENCE)

Team 1 lists this as Priority 4 gap. Team 2 identifies it as the "single largest gap" and the only Priority 1 structural recommendation. Team 3 lists it as one of three highest-priority purchase-decision blockers. All teams independently arrive at the same conclusion: the absence of Trustpilot, G2, or equivalent third-party validation is the most structurally important gap to close, because it is the gap shared by every direct competitor and addressed by all of them.

---

#### Agreement 5.3 — The methodology / statutory citation content is the site's core differentiator (HIGH CONFIDENCE)

Team 1 calls it "the primary trust signal for this buyer." Team 2 describes it as "an authentic trust signal competitors without our specificity cannot replicate." Team 3 identifies statute citations as a "human-crafted signal (Strong)" that works against AI-detection. Three teams, same conclusion from different angles. This content — the direct .gov source citations, penalty amounts, specific statute sections — must be preserved and made more prominent, not diluted.

---

#### Agreement 5.4 — Transparent one-time pricing is a competitive advantage (MODERATE-HIGH CONFIDENCE)

Team 1 (Finding 6) and Team 2 (Diagnosis section) both independently identify transparent, upfront one-time pricing as matching the correct pattern for this market tier and being a genuine strength. This finding is corroborated by the NN/G primary source (verified), which explicitly states that hiding prices creates friction and deters professional buyers.

---

#### Agreement 5.5 — The absence of verifiable social proof (reviews, customer count, testimonials) is a real gap (HIGH CONFIDENCE)

All three teams identify the complete absence of third-party validation as a meaningful trust deficit. Teams 1 and 3 note this is especially consequential because compliance buyers in regulated industries are documented as being more risk-averse and more likely to require peer validation before first-mover purchase. The point is well-supported and not in dispute.

### 6. Surprises — What Changed My Thinking

---

#### Surprise 6.1 — The Gartner stat already has a more current version

Team 1's citation of the June 2025 Gartner rep-free preference study (61%) is the most precisely cited primary source in all three reports. But in the 16 days before this expedition, Gartner published an updated version showing 67%. This is not Team 1's fault — the June 2025 citation was correct when used. But it illustrates that even well-sourced B2B research moves fast, and any published version of these findings should use the current figure.

---

#### Surprise 6.2 — The "AI detection" framing may create a different risk than intended

Team 3's analysis of AI-detection signals is thoughtful and the specific tells are mostly real. But there is a tension in the report that Team 3 did not address: trying to "not look AI-generated" by redesigning the visual surface (changing colors, removing numbered circles, varying layouts) is a different project than building trust with a compliance buyer. A compliance buyer who is not a designer will not consciously notice that the border-radius is uniform or that the numbered circles are a "default pattern." Team 3 correctly identifies this in Section 5 ("designers notice, compliance buyers less so") but then spends most of the report on tells that compliance buyers are not measuring.

The risk: acting on the full Team 3 checklist could produce a redesign that reads as "more human to designers" without meaningfully improving trust for the actual buyer. The two highest-impact items from Team 3 (human identity on About page; product evidence / document preview) are the right focus. The remaining 8 tells are real observations but they are for a different audience than the one that writes the checks.

---

#### Surprise 6.3 — The Cornell citation chain is three degrees from the original research

Team 1 cited GroupM7 citing Cornell. GroupM7 itself does not paraphrase the Cornell findings accurately — the GroupM7 article says only "customers are more likely to trust unique images... according to a study done by Cornell in 2019" with a link to the arxiv PDF, but no description of the study context (P2P secondhand marketplace). Team 1 inherited the decontextualized citation without checking the original. This is a reminder that citation chains from marketing blogs to academic research papers frequently lose the original context. The finding is directionally useful; the provenance is degraded.

## Summary Verdict

### What the expedition got right

The core conclusion of all three teams — remove stock lifestyle photos, prioritize methodology content, add third-party social proof, surface human identity — is well-supported and should be acted on. The convergence across three independent research angles strengthens confidence in the direction even where individual citations are imprecise.

The most important finding in the expedition is Team 3's: **the site presents expertise without a person, and in the compliance domain, that absence is the primary trust question a buyer asks.** Teams 1 and 2 underweighted this. It deserves Priority 1 status.

### What requires correction before acting on

| Claim | Status | Action |
|-------|--------|--------|
| Cornell 2019 study applied to B2B compliance | Misapplied context | Use as weak corroborating direction only; do not cite as B2B evidence |
| 65% AI fingerprint from aiwebsitedetector.com | Fabricated statistic | Remove entirely |
| 38% generic design / Neil Patel | Double misattribution | Remove or replace with verified Figma source if found |
| 97% buyers check website first | Unsourced in cited article | Treat as directional; cite "90% compare 2-7 websites" (DesignRush, verified) instead |
| 61% Gartner rep-free preference | Real but stale | Update to 67% (Gartner, March 2026) |
| TrustRadius 74% free trial | Real but conditional | Add qualifier: "74% of those who used a free trial" not all buyers |

### What the expedition missed

- Direct A/B evidence for compliance/legal template sites specifically
- Price-band-specific trust research ($149–$997 digital professional purchases)
- Compliance officer due diligence behavior as a documented research area
- Testing whether the site's existing documentary elements (Denver skyline, Colorado statute URLs) are already partially functioning as intended

### Design direction confidence

Despite the evidence challenges above, the directional recommendations are consistent across all three teams and hold up:

1. Remove lifestyle stock photos; replace with documentary imagery or nothing
2. Surface a human identity on the About page — even one attributed sentence
3. Create a Trustpilot or G2 profile and accumulate real reviews
4. Make statutory citations and methodology more prominent (already present, needs elevation)
5. Add a document sample/preview for at least one product
6. Fix the blog pattern — consistent low-volume human editorial voice going forward

These recommendations stand even with the evidentiary corrections above.
