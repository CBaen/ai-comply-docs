Short answer: if your company uses any AI tool in the hiring process and employs people in Illinois, yes — you probably need to care.

That's the thing about [Illinois HB3773](https://www.ilga.gov/legislation/ilcs/fulltext?DocName=077500050K2-102). It doesn't just apply to tech companies building AI. It applies to the companies *using* that AI to screen resumes, schedule interviews, or evaluate candidates. Which, at this point, is a lot of companies.

**Key Takeaways (as of March 2026)**
- [Illinois HB3773](https://www.ilga.gov/legislation/ilcs/fulltext?DocName=077500050K2-102) is **in effect now** — it amended the [Illinois Human Rights Act](https://www.ilga.gov/legislation/ilcs/fulltext?DocName=077500050K2-102) effective January 1, 2026, and applies to any employer using AI in hiring decisions involving Illinois workers.
- There is **no small business exemption** — if you use AI to screen or rank Illinois applicants, you're covered regardless of company size.
- Applicants and employees can file charges directly with the [Illinois Department of Human Rights](https://dhr.illinois.gov/), creating real enforcement risk without waiting for a regulator to notice you.

Let's break it down in a way that actually makes sense.

## What Is Illinois HB3773, Exactly?

[Illinois HB3773](https://www.ilga.gov/legislation/ilcs/fulltext?DocName=077500050K2-102) — officially an amendment to the [Illinois Human Rights Act (775 ILCS 5/2-102(L))](https://www.ilga.gov/legislation/ilcs/fulltext?DocName=077500050K2-102) — went into effect on January 1, 2026 ([P.A. 103-804](https://www.ilga.gov/legislation/publicacts/103/103-0804.htm)). It's the state's attempt to keep AI from quietly discriminating against job applicants without anyone noticing.

Think of it this way: before AI, a biased hiring manager was visible. Their decisions could be challenged. With AI, bias gets baked into an algorithm, applied at scale, and nobody's quite sure where it came from.

HB3773 is Illinois saying: we see that problem. Fix it.

## Who Does Illinois HB3773 Apply To?

The law applies to employers using AI tools to screen, rank, or evaluate applicants or employees in Illinois. The law uses the term "zip code profiling" specifically — but in practice, the law is about any AI system that factors in protected characteristics (race, gender, age, disability, national origin, etc.) when making employment decisions.

If you use:
- Resume screening software that ranks candidates
- Video interview analysis tools (like those that assess tone or facial expressions)
- Skill assessments that use AI scoring
- Any automated system that filters applicants before a human sees them

...then [HB3773](https://www.ilga.gov/legislation/ilcs/fulltext?DocName=077500050K2-102) is talking to you.

And here's the part that catches people off guard: you don't have to be headquartered in Illinois. You just have to be hiring Illinois residents, or have Illinois employees.

## What Does Illinois HB3773 Actually Require?

Here's where it gets specific. The law requires employers to:

**1. Notify applicants and employees** when AI is being used in the decision-making process. You can't just quietly run someone's resume through a scoring algorithm. They have to know.

**2. Disclose what characteristics the AI uses.** You don't have to reveal proprietary code. But you do have to tell people the general categories of factors the system considers.

**3. Allow people to request a different selection process.** If an applicant says "I don't want to be evaluated by AI," you have to provide an alternative. Full stop.

**4. Not use zip code as a proxy for protected class.** This is the one most people know about. The law explicitly prohibits using geographic data in ways that effectively filter people by race or national origin — even if that's not the intent.

**5. Keep records.** You need documentation of your AI systems, including how they work and what testing has been done to check for bias.

## What Are the Penalties for Violating HB3773?

This is where ignoring [HB3773](https://www.ilga.gov/legislation/ilcs/fulltext?DocName=077500050K2-102) gets expensive.

Employees and applicants can file charges with the [Illinois Department of Human Rights](https://dhr.illinois.gov/). If there's a finding against you, you're looking at:

- **IDHR civil penalties** ([775 ILCS 5/8A-104(K)](https://www.ilga.gov/legislation/ilcs/fulltext?DocName=077500050K8A-104)): up to $16,000 for a first violation, $42,500 for a second within 5 years, $70,000 for two or more within 7 years
- Attorney's fees paid to the plaintiff
- Actual damages (uncapped in civil court actions)
- Potential injunctive relief (meaning the state can order you to change your practices)

Note: IDHR civil penalties and civil court damages are separate tracks. IDHR sets administrative penalties through its tiered schedule. If you pursue a civil action in court, actual damages are uncapped but go through a different process. Under the [Illinois Human Rights Act](https://www.ilga.gov/legislation/ilcs/fulltext?DocName=077500050K2-102), both enforcement paths are genuinely effective. This isn't a law that sits on the books collecting dust.

## "But Our AI Vendor Handles This"

Here's the misconception that will burn you: just because your vendor said their tool is compliant doesn't mean *you* are compliant.

[HB3773](https://www.ilga.gov/legislation/ilcs/fulltext?DocName=077500050K2-102) puts obligations on the employer, not just the tool provider. You still need to:

- Conduct or review bias audits of the tools you use
- Have the required disclosures and notices ready
- Maintain your own records
- Be able to provide an alternative process if requested

Your vendor might give you audit results. They might write your disclosure language. But if you haven't put those things into your own documented compliance process, you're exposed.

## What You Actually Need to Do for HB3773 Compliance

Let's make this practical.

**Step 1: Audit your tech stack.** List every tool in your hiring process. Flag anything that makes automated decisions — about who gets screened, who gets ranked, who gets moved forward.

**Step 2: Get bias audit results.** For each AI tool, either get the vendor's published audit results or commission your own. Document that you reviewed them.

**Step 3: Write your disclosure language.** You need a notice that goes to applicants explaining that AI is being used, what factors it considers, and how they can request an alternative.

**Step 4: Set up an alternative process.** Have a plan for what happens when someone opts out.

**Step 5: Keep records.** Create documentation for your compliance process and maintain it.
