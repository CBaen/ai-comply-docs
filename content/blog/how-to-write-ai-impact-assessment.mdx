An AI impact assessment sounds like something that requires a team of consultants and a six-figure legal bill. It does not. It's a structured document that answers specific questions about your AI system — and if you know what questions to answer, you can produce something genuinely compliant without a law degree.

Here's the section-by-section walkthrough.

**Key Takeaways (as of March 2026)**
- [Colorado SB24-205](https://leg.colorado.gov/bills/sb24-205) requires a written **impact assessment** for every high-risk AI system you deploy — one per system, covering data, bias risk, human oversight, and monitoring.
- This document lives in your internal compliance files. It's not published publicly. It's what you show regulators if something goes sideways.
- The most common mistake is leaving sections blank or writing generic boilerplate — that's worse than useless under the **affirmative defense** standard.

## What Is an AI Impact Assessment Under Colorado SB24-205?

Under [Colorado SB24-205](https://leg.colorado.gov/bills/sb24-205), if you deploy a **high-risk AI system** — one that makes or substantially influences consequential decisions about Colorado residents in areas like employment, credit, housing, insurance, or healthcare — you're required to conduct an impact assessment for that system.

The assessment is a written document that demonstrates you understand what your AI system does, what risks it poses, and how you're managing those risks. It lives in your compliance files. It's not published publicly. It's what you show regulators, auditors, or a court if something goes sideways.

Think of it like a fire safety inspection report for your building. You do it before there's a fire.

## Section 1: System Description

**What goes here:** A plain-language description of the AI system — what it does, who built it, what problem it solves for your business.

**What the law actually requires:** You need to be able to describe what the system does and what category of consequential decision it affects. Which bucket does it fall into — employment, credit, housing, etc.?

**Practical tip:** If you bought this system from a vendor, most of this comes from their product documentation. Pull their spec sheet, their data sheet, their onboarding materials. Paraphrase in your own words so it reads like something your company actually understands.

**What you can skip:** You don't need to describe how the model works at a technical level. You're not required to explain neural network architecture. "This system uses machine learning to score job applicants on predicted job performance" is sufficient.

## Section 2: Data Inventory

**What goes here:** What data does the system use to make decisions? Where does that data come from?

**What the law actually requires:** You need to document the categories of data the system ingests — not every field name, but the meaningful types. Age, location, credit history, work history, demographic signals, behavioral data, etc.

**Practical tip:** Ask your vendor. This is exactly the kind of question their compliance or legal team should be able to answer. If they can't tell you what data their system uses to make decisions, that's a red flag worth documenting separately.

**What you can skip:** You don't need individual-level data records. You need categories. "The system uses applicant resume data, including employment history and education credentials" is the right level of detail.

## Section 3: Intended Use and Decision Scope

**What goes here:** Who is affected by this system's outputs? What decisions does the system make or influence?

**What the law actually requires:** A description of the intended use case and the population of people who could be subject to a consequential decision made using the system.

**Practical tip:** Be honest about the "substantially influences" piece. If the system scores candidates from 1-100 and your recruiters only interview people who score above 75, the system is substantially influencing who gets interviewed. Document that, including the threshold.

**What you can skip:** You don't need to predict every possible use case. Focus on the primary intended use and the decision it feeds into.

## Section 4: Bias and Discrimination Risk

**What goes here:** What testing has been done to evaluate whether the system produces discriminatory outcomes? What were the results?

**What the law actually requires:** This is one of the meatier requirements. You need to document known or reasonably foreseeable risks of **algorithmic discrimination** — particularly around legally protected characteristics like race, gender, age, and disability status.

**Practical tip:** Again, ask your vendor. Reputable AI vendors in high-risk domains (hiring, lending, insurance) will have conducted third-party bias audits. Get that documentation and attach it. If they haven't done any bias testing, document that they haven't, note the gap, and either require it contractually or factor it into your risk assessment.

**What you can skip:** You don't need to conduct original statistical analysis yourself. Reviewing and incorporating your vendor's testing documentation is appropriate — provided you actually read it and engage with what it says.

**Common mistake:** Leaving this section blank or writing "we rely on our vendor for this." That's not sufficient. You need to affirmatively engage with the bias risk question, even if your conclusion is "we reviewed vendor audits and found no significant disparate impact."

## Section 5: Human Oversight and Appeal Process

**What goes here:** How is a human involved in decisions made using this system? What's the process for someone to contest a decision?

**What the law actually requires:** [SB24-205](https://leg.colorado.gov/bills/sb24-205) requires that people subject to consequential AI-influenced decisions be given a way to appeal and request human review. Your impact assessment should describe what that process looks like for this system.

**Practical tip:** This doesn't have to be elaborate. "Applicants who receive a rejection may contact [contact point] to request a human review of their application" is a real process. The key is that it's documented, it's real, and someone at your company is responsible for it.

**What you can skip:** You don't need a 20-step escalation process. A clear, honest description of how a person can get a human to look at their situation is what's required.

## Section 6: Ongoing Monitoring Plan

**What goes here:** How will you ensure the system continues to operate as intended over time?

**What the law actually requires:** The spirit of [SB24-205](https://leg.colorado.gov/bills/sb24-205) is that compliance is ongoing, not a one-time checkbox. Your impact assessment should describe how you'll monitor the system's outputs for accuracy, bias drift, and continued alignment with your intended use.

**Practical tip:** "We will review quarterly reports from our vendor and conduct an internal review of the system annually" is a legitimate monitoring plan. Write down what you'll actually do, not what sounds impressive.

**What you can skip:** You don't need real-time monitoring dashboards or an AI ethics committee. Reasonable periodic review, documented in writing, with someone assigned to it, is sufficient.

## How Often Do You Update an AI Impact Assessment?

Whenever something material changes. That includes:

- **Changes to the AI system itself** — if your vendor releases a significant model update, you should review whether your assessment still reflects reality
- **Changes in how you're using it** — if you expand to a new use case or a new population, update the assessment
- **Significant failures or incidents** — if the system produces outputs that are clearly wrong or discriminatory, document the incident and your response
- **Annually at minimum** — as a general rule, an untouched assessment more than 12 months old should be reviewed

The law expects living documentation, not a document you produce once and file in a drawer.

## The Most Common AI Impact Assessment Mistakes

**1. Treating it as a formality.** An impact assessment that says "we use AI responsibly" without specifics is worse than useless — it demonstrates you went through the motions without engaging. Real documentation shows you actually thought about the risks.

**2. Not getting information from your vendor.** Almost everything in a good impact assessment comes from your AI vendor. If you're writing this document without their input, you're guessing. Request their documentation formally so you have a paper trail showing you asked.

**3. Conflating developer and deployer responsibilities.** Your vendor's documentation doesn't substitute for your own assessment. You're documenting *your* use of the system — the decisions it feeds into in your business, your oversight process, your appeals process.

**4. Doing it once and forgetting it.** The affirmative defense that makes [SB24-205](https://leg.colorado.gov/bills/sb24-205) compliance worthwhile requires that you have an *active* risk management program. An outdated assessment from 2026 sitting in a folder won't protect you in 2028.
