The civil penalty under [Colorado SB24-205](https://leg.colorado.gov/bills/sb24-205) is up to **$20,000 per violation** at the Attorney General's discretion ([C.R.S. § 6-1-112(1)(a)](https://colorado.public.law/statutes/crs_6-1-112)). And "per violation" means per affected person. If your AI system improperly handled employment decisions for 50 Colorado applicants, you're not looking at $20,000 — you're looking at $1,000,000 in maximum exposure. Before legal fees.

That's the bad news. The good news is that the law also includes a provision that can substantially protect you if you've done the compliance work. Let's go through both sides.

**Key Takeaways (as of March 2026)**
- Civil penalties under [Colorado SB24-205](https://leg.colorado.gov/bills/sb24-205) reach up to **$20,000 per affected individual** at the AG's discretion ([C.R.S. § 6-1-112(1)(a)](https://colorado.public.law/statutes/crs_6-1-112)) — the per-person math escalates fast. Violations involving persons age 60+ carry penalties up to $50,000.
- The **Colorado Attorney General** has exclusive enforcement authority; there is no private right of action under this statute.
- The law includes an **affirmative defense**: documented, good-faith compliance can substantially limit your liability even if something goes wrong.

## Who Enforces Colorado SB24-205?

The **Colorado Attorney General** has primary enforcement authority over [SB24-205](https://leg.colorado.gov/bills/sb24-205). Under [C.R.S. §§ 6-1-1701 through 6-1-1707](https://leg.colorado.gov/bills/sb24-205), the AG can investigate suspected violations, issue civil investigative demands, and bring civil actions against non-compliant developers and deployers.

There is no private right of action built directly into SB24-205 itself — meaning individuals can't sue you directly under this specific statute the way they can under some other laws. Enforcement runs through the AG's office.

That said, "the AG has to come find me" is a riskier bet than it sounds.

## How Colorado AI Law Investigations Get Triggered

The AG doesn't have a surveillance system monitoring every AI deployment in Colorado. In practice, investigations tend to start because:

**Complaints from individuals.** Someone believes an AI system made an unfair decision about them — they didn't get a job, were denied a loan, got a worse insurance rate — and they report it. The AG investigates.

**Pattern complaints.** A cluster of similar complaints from similarly situated people draws attention. A bias advocacy organization notices that a particular hiring tool seems to screen out candidates with certain characteristics at unusual rates and brings it forward.

**Incident reporting.** [SB24-205](https://leg.colorado.gov/bills/sb24-205) requires notification when certain failures or discriminatory outcomes are discovered. A company that self-reports can shape the narrative. A company that conceals and gets caught is in a worse position.

**Media attention.** This sounds obvious, but a single news story about an AI discrimination incident is enough to prompt regulatory scrutiny. If your system makes a decision that ends up in the news, expect contact.

## The Colorado SB24-205 Penalty Math

Here's where the numbers get uncomfortable.

The statute sets civil penalties at up to **$20,000 per violation** ([C.R.S. § 6-1-112(1)(a)](https://colorado.public.law/statutes/crs_6-1-112)). The definition of "violation" is tied to each affected individual. If your AI system improperly influenced a decision about a person without the required disclosures, notices, or oversight — that's one violation per person. Note: violations involving persons age 60 or older carry enhanced penalties up to $50,000 per violation ([C.R.S. § 6-1-112(1)(c)](https://colorado.public.law/statutes/crs_6-1-112)).

Let's run some scenarios:

**Small exposure**: You use an AI hiring tool that screened 40 Colorado applicants this year without the required notices. 40 violations × $20,000 = **$800,000 maximum exposure.**

**Medium exposure**: You run an AI-assisted insurance underwriting process that touched 200 Colorado policyholders in the past 12 months without proper impact assessments or disclosure. 200 violations × $20,000 = **$4,000,000 maximum exposure.**

**Large exposure**: You operate a platform that uses AI to make credit decisions for Colorado customers. 1,000 applicants processed. **$20,000,000 maximum exposure.**

The word "maximum" is doing a lot of work in those sentences. Actual penalties will depend on the AG's discretion, the severity of harm, your cooperation, and whether the violation was willful. In practice, settlements typically come in below the statutory maximum.

But here's the thing about maximums: they define the ceiling of your risk, and your negotiating leverage in any enforcement proceeding depends heavily on what you can show you did beforehand.

## The Affirmative Defense: Your Documentation Is Your Protection

This is the provision that makes [SB24-205](https://leg.colorado.gov/bills/sb24-205) different from most compliance regimes, and it's the most important thing most businesses don't know about.

SB24-205 includes an **affirmative defense** for deployers. If a violation is alleged, you can defend yourself by demonstrating that at the time of the alleged violation, you had:

- A reasonable AI risk management program in place
- Impact assessments completed for your high-risk AI systems
- Appropriate notices and appeals processes for affected individuals
- Evidence that you were using this program in good faith

What this means in practical terms: the legal question isn't just "did your AI system produce a discriminatory outcome?" It becomes "did you take reasonable steps to prevent that outcome, and did you have documentation showing you were operating in good faith?"

A company with no documentation has no affirmative defense. A company with comprehensive documentation — a real risk management policy, completed impact assessments, written notice processes — has a meaningful argument even if something went wrong.

The law is explicitly acknowledging that AI systems aren't perfect. It's not trying to hold you liable for every failure. It's trying to hold you liable for failures you made no effort to prevent.

## What "Good Faith" Documentation Looks Like

The affirmative defense isn't a magic shield. You can't write one paragraph titled "AI Policy" and claim you have a risk management program. Regulators and courts will look at whether your documentation was:

**Substantive.** Does it actually engage with the risks your specific AI system poses? Or is it generic boilerplate that could apply to any company?

**Implemented.** Did you actually follow your documented processes? If your policy says you conduct annual reviews and you've never done one, the policy undermines you more than it helps.

**Current.** Is the documentation up to date? An impact assessment from two years ago that doesn't reflect how you're currently using the system isn't evidence of a good-faith ongoing program. It's evidence you did the work once and stopped.

**Proportionate.** Does the level of documentation match the risk? High-risk AI systems require substantive assessments. You can't use a three-sentence summary to cover a system that's making credit decisions for thousands of people.

## "We Didn't Know About This Law" Is Not a Defense

Not a defense. The statute doesn't require the AG to prove you knew about [SB24-205](https://leg.colorado.gov/bills/sb24-205) before finding a violation. Ignorance of the law has never been a legal defense, and that hasn't changed here.

What's more, the AG's office published guidance. The law passed in 2024 and takes effect June 30, 2026. The argument that you had no reasonable way to know about a law that's been publicly debated and documented for two years is not a winning argument.

## The Actual Cost Comparison: Compliance vs. Non-Compliance

Here's the math that matters for most small businesses:

**Cost of compliance documentation**: Getting your impact assessments, risk management policy, consumer notices, and appeals process documentation in order runs in the hundreds to low thousands of dollars, depending on how many AI systems you're assessing and whether you use a documentation service or engage attorneys directly.

**Cost of non-compliance**: AG investigation, civil penalties up to $20,000 per affected person, legal fees to defend the action, potential reputational damage, and the cost of emergency remediation — all of which comes due after the fact, under adversarial conditions.

The framing of "is compliance worth it" assumes compliance is the expensive option. It isn't. Non-compliance is the expensive option. Compliance is just paying for the insurance before the accident.

## What To Do Before the June 30, 2026 Deadline

If you're in scope under [SB24-205](https://leg.colorado.gov/bills/sb24-205) — meaning you deploy high-risk AI systems that affect Colorado residents — here's the priority order:

1. **Identify your high-risk AI systems.** Every tool that makes or substantially influences employment, credit, housing, insurance, healthcare, or education decisions for Colorado residents.
2. **Complete impact assessments.** One per system, covering what it does, what data it uses, bias risk, human oversight, and your monitoring plan. See our guide: [How to Write an AI Impact Assessment](/blog/how-to-write-ai-impact-assessment)
3. **Implement your risk management program.** A written policy that describes how you govern AI use across your organization.
4. **Build your notice and appeals processes.** Affected individuals need to know AI was used in decisions about them and how to request human review.
5. **Document everything.** The affirmative defense is only available to companies that can show their work.

The deadline is June 30, 2026. That's real. The penalties are real. The defense is also real — and it's available to any company that does the work.
