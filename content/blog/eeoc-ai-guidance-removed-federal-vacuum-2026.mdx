We went to the EEOC's AI guidance page this week during routine research. The page at eeoc.gov/ai returned a 404. We tried the Algorithmic Fairness Initiative page. Also a 404. We tried the adverse impact guidance on AI and employment selection. 404.

Every piece of AI-specific employment guidance the EEOC published is gone. We verified this on March 21, 2026. The only thing still standing is a hub page about AI and disability law — and even that page's underlying guidance documents have been deleted. The rest has been removed without announcement.

This is not a small administrative change. If your company uses AI in hiring, performance reviews, or any other employment decision — and if you built any part of your compliance program on the assumption that there was a federal framework governing this — you need to understand what just happened.

## What We Found

The EEOC's AI page at eeoc.gov/ai now returns a 404 error. The Algorithmic Fairness Initiative page, which the agency launched in October 2021 to formally study AI's impact on employment decisions, returns a 404. The technical guidance document titled "Select Issues: Assessing Adverse Impact in Software, Algorithms, and Artificial Intelligence Used in Employment Selection Procedures Under Title VII of the Civil Rights Act of 1964" — the closest thing to a federal technical standard for evaluating AI hiring tools — returns a 404.

One page technically survives: ["Artificial Intelligence and the ADA"](https://www.eeoc.gov/eeoc-disability-related-resources/artificial-intelligence-and-ada), which was intended to address how AI intersects with disability discrimination under the Americans with Disabilities Act. But even this page is functionally gutted — the detailed ADA guidance document it links to returns a 404, the Tips for Workers resource returns a 404, and the link to the Algorithmic Fairness Initiative returns a 404. What remains is a landing page with broken links and a YouTube video.

That is the entirety of what remains. No adverse impact framework. No guidance on race, sex, age, national origin, or any other class protected under Title VII or the ADEA.

The federal AI employment compliance floor has been removed.

## What Was There Before

The EEOC's Algorithmic Fairness Initiative was launched in October 2021 with explicit recognition that AI tools were reshaping how employers make decisions and that existing civil rights law applied to those tools. Over the following two years, the initiative produced real, usable guidance.

The adverse impact document explained how employers and vendors should evaluate whether an AI hiring tool was producing discriminatory outcomes — not just in intent, but in effect. It explained how to calculate selection rates across demographic groups, what statistical thresholds signal a compliance problem, and how employers should assess their exposure when they rely on third-party AI products.

This was the federal anchor for employer AI compliance programs. Many employers used it as the starting point for vendor due diligence. Many compliance policies cited it. Many legal teams organized their AI governance frameworks around its technical framework.

That framework is now gone. It did not sunset. It did not expire. It was deleted.

If your business uses AI in hiring and you want to understand what these tools mean for your existing state-law exposure, our post on [why your hiring software probably counts as AI under state law](/blog/hiring-software-uses-ai-employment-law-compliance) is a good place to start.

## Why This Matters Now

The timing could not be worse for employers trying to build a coherent compliance posture. Two state AI laws are already in effect — Texas TRAIGA ([HB 149](https://capitol.texas.gov/BillLookup/Text.aspx?LegSess=89R&Bill=HB149), effective January 1, 2026) and Illinois HB 3773 ([Illinois Human Rights Act, as amended](https://www.ilga.gov/legislation/BillStatus.asp?DocNum=3773&GAID=17&DocTypeID=HB&SessionID=112), effective January 1, 2026) — and a third, Colorado SB 24-205 ([C.R.S. § 6-1-1701 et seq.](https://leg.colorado.gov/bills/sb24-205)), takes effect June 30, 2026.

Each of these laws imposes obligations relevant to employers who use AI. Each is real, enforceable law. And each defines the problem — and the solution — differently.

This is the central issue. Before these laws existed, an employer could look to the EEOC's adverse impact framework as a single federal standard and build a compliance program around it. Run your AI tools through an adverse impact analysis, document the results, fix the problems. That logic held across jurisdictions because federal civil rights law applied everywhere.

That logic still holds for the ADA. But for everything else, the federal anchor is gone.

## The Standards Gap

The state laws that have moved into the federal vacuum do not agree on what discrimination means in the context of AI.

**Illinois** treats AI hiring discrimination as a civil rights violation under the Illinois Human Rights Act ([775 ILCS 5/2-102](https://www.ilga.gov/legislation/BillStatus.asp?DocNum=3773&GAID=17&DocTypeID=HB&SessionID=112)). The standard is disparate impact — it is a violation if an employer uses AI "that has the effect of" discriminating on the basis of a protected class. Intent is irrelevant. Outcome is what the statute looks at. This is closest to what the EEOC's removed guidance contemplated. For the full breakdown of what Illinois requires, see our [Illinois HB3773 compliance guide](/blog/illinois-hb3773-ai-employment-law-what-employers-need).

**Texas** takes a fundamentally different position. Under TRAIGA Sec. 552.056 ([HB 149](https://capitol.texas.gov/BillLookup/Text.aspx?LegSess=89R&Bill=HB149)), it is unlawful to develop or deploy an AI system "with the intent to unlawfully discriminate against a protected class in violation of state or federal law." This is a general anti-discrimination prohibition — it applies to AI in employment, but also in lending, insurance, healthcare, and any other context where state or federal law prohibits discrimination. Disparate impact — the fact that an AI tool produces outcomes that disproportionately harm a protected class — is explicitly insufficient on its own to establish a violation. This is a meaningfully different legal theory than what the EEOC's adverse impact guidance was built around. See our [Texas TRAIGA compliance guide](/blog/texas-traiga-hb149-ai-law-compliance-guide) for the full picture.

**Colorado** uses a third approach — a "reasonable care" standard for deployers of high-risk AI systems making "consequential decisions" ([C.R.S. § 6-1-1701 et seq.](https://leg.colorado.gov/bills/sb24-205)). The law is a consumer protection statute, not an employment statute — but employment is explicitly one of the consequential decision domains it covers, alongside lending, healthcare, housing, insurance, education, and legal services. Deployers must use reasonable care to protect consumers from algorithmic discrimination. This is closer to a negligence framework: you can be liable not because you intended to discriminate, and not purely because of a discriminatory outcome, but because you failed to take reasonable precautions. Colorado's law takes effect June 30, 2026, after being [delayed from February 1, 2026 by SB 25B-004](https://leg.colorado.gov/bills/sb25b-004) during a special legislative session in August 2025.

Three states. Three legal theories. No federal baseline to unify them.

An employer using AI hiring tools in all three states now needs to simultaneously satisfy an effects test (Illinois), avoid evidence of intent (Texas), and demonstrate reasonable care (Colorado). These standards are not necessarily incompatible, but they require different documentation, different audit approaches, and different vendor conversations.

The EEOC's adverse impact framework would have provided at least a portable technical methodology — something that said: here is how you evaluate whether an AI tool discriminates, here is what the math looks like, here is what a defensible audit produces. That methodology is gone from the federal level. The state laws reference their own standards but do not replace the technical depth of the EEOC's technical resource.

## What Employers Should Do

The absence of federal guidance is not a reason to stop building a compliance program. It is a reason to build one that is portable across the state standards that now fill the gap.

**Document everything about every AI tool you use in employment decisions.** What the tool does, what data it uses, what outputs it produces, and how those outputs influence actual decisions. This documentation is the foundation under every state standard — it is what "reasonable care" looks like in Colorado, it is what Illinois requires before a violation can be assessed, and it is what any employer would need to defend against a discrimination claim under any standard.

**Conduct vendor due diligence in writing.** Ask every AI vendor what bias testing they perform, what demographic data they use, what their impact ratio results show, and what they will provide if a regulator asks. Get the answers in writing. A vendor assessment framework built around the disclosure questions that state laws are most likely to surface should cover adverse impact calculations, demographic breakdowns, and remediation plans.

**Know which state laws apply to you.** This is not a question of where your company is headquartered. Illinois's law applies if you have employees in Illinois. Texas's law applies to covered AI deployments in Texas. Colorado's law applies to consequential decisions affecting Colorado consumers — and employment is one of those domains. If you operate in multiple states, you have multiple obligations. Our post on [building an AI governance framework that works across state laws](/blog/ai-governance-framework-checklist-every-state-law) walks through how to structure that.

**Use the NIST AI Risk Management Framework as a portable technical anchor.** The NIST AI RMF is not law — it is a voluntary framework published by the National Institute of Standards and Technology. But it is technically rigorous, it is portable across jurisdictions, and it is the closest thing to a neutral federal technical standard that still exists. Both Colorado and Texas reference NIST-aligned frameworks as relevant considerations for compliance. Building your AI governance program around the NIST RMF means you have a defensible methodology to point to regardless of which state's standard is being applied. See our [NIST AI RMF compliance guide](/blog/nist-ai-rmf-compliance-guide-colorado-legal-shield) for a practical breakdown.

**Get state-specific compliance documents in place now.** Illinois requires employer notice to employees and applicants that AI is being used in employment decisions ([775 ILCS 5/2-102](https://www.ilga.gov/legislation/BillStatus.asp?DocNum=3773&GAID=17&DocTypeID=HB&SessionID=112)). Colorado requires impact assessments for high-risk systems ([C.R.S. § 6-1-1701](https://leg.colorado.gov/bills/sb24-205)). Texas requires documentation demonstrating that any AI use was not intentionally discriminatory. These are distinct documents with distinct purposes.

The federal guidance vacuum is real. What fills it is the combination of your documentation, your audit records, your vendor agreements, and your state-law compliance materials. That combination is what protects you when there is no unified federal standard to point to.
