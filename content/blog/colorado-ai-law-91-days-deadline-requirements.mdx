Colorado's SB 24-205, as amended by [SB 25B-004](https://leg.colorado.gov/bills/sb25b-004), imposes enforceable obligations on developers and deployers of high-risk artificial intelligence systems beginning June 30, 2026. Businesses that use AI in consequential decisions about employment, housing, credit, health care, insurance, education, or legal services must have risk management policies, impact assessments, consumer notices, and disclosure frameworks in place by that date or face civil penalties of up to $20,000 per violation under the Colorado Consumer Protection Act.

## What is Colorado's AI law and what changed when SB 25B-004 was signed?

[Colorado SB 24-205](https://leg.colorado.gov/bills/sb24-205) creates C.R.S. §§ 6-1-1701 through 6-1-1707 — the nation's first comprehensive state framework preventing algorithmic discrimination in high-risk AI systems. [SB 25B-004](https://leg.colorado.gov/bills/sb25b-004), signed August 28, 2025, made exactly one structural change: replacing every instance of "February 1, 2026" with "June 30, 2026" across three statutory sections. No definitions, enforcement provisions, or affirmative defense requirements were modified.

The Colorado General Assembly passed [SB 24-205](https://leg.colorado.gov/bills/sb24-205), "Consumer Protections for Artificial Intelligence," during its 2024 regular session. Governor Polis signed it on May 17, 2024. The law creates Part 17 of Article 1 of Title 6 of the Colorado Revised Statutes (C.R.S. §§ 6-1-1701 through 6-1-1707), establishing what is now the nation's first comprehensive state framework aimed at preventing algorithmic discrimination in high-risk AI systems.

The original law set February 1, 2026, as the date obligations would take effect. That changed during an August 2025 extraordinary session. [SB 25B-004](https://leg.colorado.gov/bills/sb25b-004), "Increase Transparency for Algorithmic Systems," was introduced on August 21, 2025, passed both chambers, and was signed by the Governor on August 28, 2025.

SB 25B-004 made one structural change: it replaced every instance of "February 1, 2026" with "June 30, 2026" across three sections of the statute — C.R.S. § 6-1-1702 (developer duties), § 6-1-1703 (deployer duties), and § 6-1-1704 (consumer disclosure). No changes were made to the definitions, enforcement, affirmative defense, permitted activities, or rulemaking provisions.

## Who does Colorado SB 24-205 cover — and what counts as a high-risk AI system?

Under [C.R.S. § 6-1-1701](https://leg.colorado.gov/bills/sb24-205), the law covers developers (businesses that build or substantially modify AI systems in Colorado) and deployers (businesses that use high-risk AI systems in Colorado). It applies exclusively to "high-risk" systems — those that make or substantially factor into consequential decisions about education, employment, financial services, essential government services, healthcare, housing, insurance, or legal services.

Under C.R.S. § 6-1-1701, a "developer" is a person doing business in Colorado that develops or intentionally and substantially modifies an AI system. A "deployer" is a person doing business in Colorado that deploys (uses) a high-risk AI system. A "consumer" is defined as a Colorado resident.

The law applies exclusively to "high-risk" AI systems: those that make, or are a substantial factor in making, a "consequential decision." The statute defines consequential decisions as those affecting education, employment, financial or lending services, essential government services, health-care services, housing, insurance, or legal services (§ 6-1-1701(2)).

## Is there any chance Colorado's AI law will be amended or delayed before the June 30, 2026 deadline?

No amending bill has been introduced in the [2026 Regular Session](https://leg.colorado.gov/bills/bill-search?q=%22artificial+intelligence%22&sort=Most+Relevant&page=1), and the General Assembly adjourns sine die on May 13, 2026. The legislature has failed to revise the core law four times — [SB 25-318](https://leg.colorado.gov/bills/sb25-318), HB 25B-1009, and SB 25B-008 were all killed in committee, and only the date-extension bill survived. Do not build a compliance plan around the assumption of legislative relief.

A search of the [Colorado General Assembly bill database](https://leg.colorado.gov/bills/bill-search?q=%22artificial+intelligence%22&sort=Most+Relevant&page=1) for "artificial intelligence" and "algorithmic" across the 2026 Regular Session reveals that no bill has been introduced to amend, revise, delay, or repeal Part 17.

The legislature has had four opportunities to substantively revise the AI law, and none has produced a revision to the law's core requirements:

- [SB 25-318](https://leg.colorado.gov/bills/sb25-318), "Artificial Intelligence Consumer Protections" — indefinitely postponed by a 5-2 vote in the Senate Committee on Business, Labor, & Technology on May 5, 2025

- SB 25B-008, "Tech-Neutral Anti-Discrimination Clarification Act" — indefinitely postponed during the August 2025 extraordinary session
- 2026 Regular Session — zero amending bills introduced

The General Assembly is scheduled to adjourn sine die on May 13, 2026. With no amending bill introduced and the session winding down, the probability of legislative relief before June 30 is diminishing.

## What are the civil penalties for violating Colorado SB 24-205's AI requirements?

Violations are treated as deceptive trade practices under the [Colorado Consumer Protection Act](https://leg.colorado.gov/bills/sb24-205) via C.R.S. § 6-1-1706(2). Under C.R.S. § 6-1-112(1)(a), the penalty is up to $20,000 per violation — and the statute specifies each violation constitutes a separate violation with respect to each consumer. A single non-compliant AI system affecting 1,000 consumers creates exposure of up to $20 million. Violations involving consumers age 60 or older increase to $50,000 per violation.

Violations of the AI law are routed through the [Colorado Consumer Protection Act](https://leg.colorado.gov/bills/sb24-205) via C.R.S. § 6-1-1706(2) and § 6-1-105. Under [C.R.S. § 6-1-112(1)(a)](https://leg.colorado.gov/bills/sb24-205), the AG or a district attorney may bring a civil action, and any person who violates any provision shall pay a civil penalty of not more than $20,000 for each violation. Where the violation involves a consumer who is 60 years of age or older, the maximum penalty increases to $50,000 per violation ([C.R.S. § 6-1-112(1)(c)](https://leg.colorado.gov/bills/sb24-205)).

The statute specifies that a violation constitutes a separate violation with respect to each consumer. A single non-compliant AI system affecting 1,000 consumers creates theoretical exposure of up to $20 million. There is no private right of action — enforcement is AG-exclusive under § 6-1-1706(1).

## What is the affirmative defense under Colorado SB 24-205, and how do businesses qualify for it?

The affirmative defense under [§ 6-1-1706(3)](https://leg.colorado.gov/bills/sb24-205) requires two conditions: (1) compliance with a nationally or internationally recognized AI risk management framework designated by the Act or the Attorney General, and (2) active measures to discover and correct violations. Both must be met — framework compliance alone is insufficient. The [NIST AI RMF](https://airc.nist.gov/) and ISO/IEC 42001 are the most prominent frameworks practitioners expect to qualify.

The statute provides an affirmative defense under § 6-1-1706(3) for businesses that meet both conditions:

1. **Comply with a nationally or internationally recognized AI risk management framework** designated by the act or the Attorney General
2. **Take measures to discover and correct violations** of the act

The statute does not name the NIST AI Risk Management Framework specifically — it references "a nationally or internationally recognized risk management framework for artificial intelligence systems." The [NIST AI RMF](https://airc.nist.gov/) and ISO/IEC 42001 are the most prominent frameworks that fit this description.

Both conditions must be met. Framework compliance alone is not sufficient without the discovery-and-correction mechanism.

## How does Colorado SB 24-205 compare to AI compliance laws now in effect in other states?

Colorado is ahead of but not alone in the AI governance landscape: [Texas HB 149 (TRAIGA)](https://capitol.texas.gov/tlodocs/89R/billtext/html/HB00149F.htm) took effect January 1, 2026 with penalties up to $200,000 per uncurable violation; [Illinois Public Act 103-0804](https://www.ilga.gov/Legislation/publicacts/view/103-0804) also took effect January 1, 2026 prohibiting discriminatory AI in employment; and [California's CPPA ADMT regulations](https://cppa.ca.gov/announcements/2025/20250923.html) required risk assessments as of January 1, 2026 with consumer notice requirements beginning January 1, 2027.

Colorado is not alone, but it is ahead of the curve:

- **Texas**: [HB 149 (TRAIGA)](https://capitol.texas.gov/tlodocs/89R/billtext/html/HB00149F.htm) — signed June 22, 2025, effective January 1, 2026. Covers all AI deployers and developers in Texas with no high-risk carveout.
- **Illinois**: [Public Act 103-0804](https://www.ilga.gov/Legislation/publicacts/view/103-0804) — effective January 1, 2026. Amends the Illinois Human Rights Act to prohibit discriminatory AI in employment and require employee notification.
- **California**: [CPPA ADMT regulations](https://cppa.ca.gov/announcements/2025/20250923.html) — risk assessments required January 1, 2026. Consumer ADMT notice and opt-out requirements begin January 1, 2027.
