You might be completely off the hook. Let's find out — because the answer is actually more specific than most people realize, and a lot of businesses panic unnecessarily while others who should be paying attention aren't.

Colorado SB24-205, the Colorado Artificial Intelligence Act, applies to a specific category of AI use. Not every business that has ever touched an AI tool. Not every business in Colorado. Let's walk through this in order.

## Step 1: Do You Have Any Connection to Colorado?

The law applies to businesses that deploy high-risk AI systems affecting **Colorado residents** — as customers, employees, or applicants. You don't have to be headquartered in Colorado. You don't have to have a Colorado office.

If you have Colorado customers or Colorado employees, the law reaches you regardless of where you're based.

If you have absolutely zero Colorado presence — no customers there, no employees there, no applicants from there — you're not in scope and you can stop reading.

Still here? Good. On to step 2.

## Step 2: Are You a Developer or a Deployer?

SB24-205 covers two types of companies, and which one you are matters.

**Developer**: You built the AI system. You wrote the model, trained it, and sell or license it to others.

**Deployer**: You took someone else's AI system and used it in your business to make decisions about people.

Most small and mid-size businesses are **deployers**. You didn't build anything. You signed up for an AI-powered hiring tool, or a loan underwriting service, or an AI scheduling platform. That makes you a deployer.

Both developers and deployers have obligations under SB24-205, but the specifics differ. This post focuses on deployers, because that's most of you.

## Step 3: Does Your AI System Qualify as "High-Risk"?

Here's where most businesses get confused — and where many businesses realize they're actually off the hook.

The law doesn't apply to every use of AI. It specifically applies to **high-risk AI systems**, which are systems that make or substantially influence consequential decisions about people's lives.

The statute identifies these decision areas as high-risk:

- **Employment** — hiring, firing, pay, performance evaluation, promotion
- **Credit and lending** — loan approvals, credit scores, financial product eligibility
- **Housing** — rental decisions, property management
- **Insurance** — underwriting, claims, coverage decisions
- **Healthcare** — medical treatment, diagnosis assistance, care management
- **Education** — enrollment decisions, academic program eligibility
- **Legal proceedings** — risk assessment for bail, parole, sentencing

If you're using AI to help make those kinds of decisions about Colorado residents, you're probably in scope.

If your AI use is a chatbot that answers customer service questions, a tool that summarizes your email, or a system that generates marketing copy — that's not a high-risk decision under this law. You're likely not in scope.

### The "Substantially Influence" Trap

Note that the law doesn't require the AI to make the final call. If the AI's output **substantially influences** a human decision in one of those categories, the system still qualifies as high-risk.

Your hiring manager technically makes the offer, but if they're ranking candidates using an AI scoring tool, that tool is high-risk. Don't let the human-in-the-loop framing give you false comfort.

## Step 4: Does the Small Deployer Situation Apply to You?

SB24-205 doesn't have a hard employee-count cutoff that simply exempts small businesses. But the law does acknowledge that obligations should be **"reasonable"** relative to your size, resources, and the nature of your AI use.

A 10-person startup has different compliance expectations than a regional insurance company. You're still expected to have a risk management program and conduct impact assessments — but what those look like can be proportional to your scale.

This is actually good news: you don't need the same elaborate compliance infrastructure as a Fortune 500. You need documentation that's real, written, and reflects what you actually do.

## Quick Decision Tree Summary

Let's put it together:

**Not in scope** if any of these are true:
- You have no Colorado customers, employees, or applicants
- You use AI only for content creation, summarization, customer service chat, or internal productivity
- Your AI tool makes suggestions that humans ignore, with no real influence on outcomes

**Probably in scope** if all of these are true:
- You have Colorado customers, employees, or applicants
- You use an AI system that makes or substantially influences decisions in employment, credit, housing, insurance, healthcare, education, or legal contexts
- That AI system's output actually affects real decisions about real people

**Gray area**: If your AI use touches one of those categories but plays a minor supporting role, you may need a legal read on whether the system is "substantially" influencing decisions. When in doubt, document — the affirmative defense rewards companies that make good-faith efforts.

## If You're In Scope: What Comes Next

Being in scope isn't a disaster. It means you have some work to do before June 30, 2026. Specifically:

1. **Document your risk management program.** A written policy describing how you identify, assess, and monitor AI risk.
2. **Conduct impact assessments** for each high-risk AI system you deploy.
3. **Set up consumer notices** so affected Colorado residents know AI was used in decisions about them.
4. **Establish an appeals process** so people can request human review.

The law also includes an affirmative defense: if you're sued or investigated and you can show you had a reasonable risk management program in place and were following it in good faith, that documentation can protect you even if something went wrong.

In other words, doing the work — and writing it down — is the entire ballgame.
