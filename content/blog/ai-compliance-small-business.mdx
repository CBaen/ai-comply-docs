There's a story going around that AI compliance is only for Big Tech — Google, OpenAI, the companies actually building the scary robots.

This story is comforting and mostly wrong.

Here's the thing: most AI compliance laws target the businesses *using* AI, not just the ones building it. That means the small consulting firm that uses an AI scheduling tool. The regional insurance company using AI to assess claims. The 50-person manufacturer using AI to screen job applicants.

**TL;DR**
- If you use AI tools that make decisions about people (hiring, lending, insurance), you are a **deployer** — and deployer obligations are real regardless of company size.
- [Illinois HB3773](/regulations/illinois-hb3773) is in effect now. [Colorado SB24-205](/regulations/colorado-sb24-205) kicks in June 30, 2026.
- Having documentation in place is your best — and cheapest — protection against enforcement.

Let's sort out what actually applies to you.

## What Is the Developer vs. Deployer Distinction in AI Law?

This is the most important concept in AI compliance law right now, and almost nobody explains it clearly.

**Developer** — The company that builds the AI system. Think OpenAI building GPT, or HireVue building their video interview analysis tool.

**Deployer** — The company that takes that AI and uses it in their business to make decisions about people. That's you, if you're licensing an AI tool from a vendor.

For most small businesses, you're a deployer. You didn't build anything. You signed up for software.

Here's why that matters: **most state AI laws impose obligations on deployers, not just developers.** You bought someone else's product, but you're still responsible for how it affects your employees and customers.

Think of it like food safety. You didn't grow the lettuce, but if you serve contaminated lettuce in your restaurant, that's your problem.

## Which AI Laws Apply to Small Businesses?

Let's go state by state on the ones that are actually in effect or coming soon, as of March 2026.

### Does Illinois HB3773 Apply to Small Businesses?

[Illinois HB3773](https://www.ilga.gov/legislation/ilcs/documents/077500050K2-102.htm) — an amendment to the [Illinois Human Rights Act (775 ILCS 5/2-102)](https://www.ilga.gov/legislation/ilcs/documents/077500050K2-102.htm) — is in effect now. It applies to any employer using AI tools in hiring or employment decisions involving Illinois workers.

**Small business threshold**: The law doesn't exempt small businesses. If you have Illinois employees and you use any AI tool to screen, rank, or evaluate them, you're covered.

**What you need**: Written notices to applicants, bias audit documentation, an opt-out process.

See our full breakdown: [What Is Illinois HB3773 and Do I Need to Care?](/blog/what-is-illinois-hb3773)

### Does Colorado SB24-205 Apply to Small Businesses?

[Colorado SB24-205](https://leg.colorado.gov/bills/sb24-205) — the Colorado Artificial Intelligence Act — takes effect **June 30, 2026**. It focuses on "high-risk AI systems," which it defines as systems that make consequential decisions about people in areas like employment, housing, insurance, credit, and healthcare.

**Small business threshold**: The law covers "developers" and "deployers" without a small business exemption — but the obligations are scaled to what's "reasonable" given your size and resources. A 10-person company has different obligations than a 10,000-person company. But you still have obligations.

**What you need**: An AI governance policy, impact assessments for high-risk systems, and notice to affected individuals.

See our full guide: [Colorado SB24-205: What You Actually Need to Do](/blog/colorado-sb24-205-guide)

### Texas TRAIGA (Coming Soon)

Texas's Responsible AI Governance Act is still working its way through the process, but it's expected to follow a similar developer/deployer structure with some carve-outs for smaller businesses.

### California (Various)

California has been layering AI requirements into existing laws like [CCPA](https://oag.ca.gov/privacy/ccpa) and [FEHA](https://calcivilrights.ca.gov/feha/). If you have California customers or employees, you likely have some obligations already. This one's genuinely complicated and warrants a separate deep-dive.

## What AI Laws Don't Apply to Small Businesses?

There are some exemptions worth knowing about.

**Federal AI oversight**: The current federal AI regulatory landscape is light and mostly focused on government use and high-risk sectors (healthcare, financial services). Most small businesses don't interact with it.

**[NIST AI Risk Management Framework](https://www.nist.gov/itl/ai-risk-management-framework)**: This is a voluntary framework, not law. You don't have to comply with it. (Though following it doesn't hurt.)

**[EU AI Act](https://digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu/en/policies/regulatory-framework-ai)**: This applies to companies operating in the EU market. If you're strictly domestic, it's not your problem right now.

## What Do Small Businesses Actually Need to Do for AI Compliance?

For most small businesses, the compliance answer comes down to four things:

**1. Know your AI tools**

Make a list. Every tool you use that makes automated decisions about people — hiring, scheduling, performance, lending, pricing — belongs on this list. Be honest. "We just use ChatGPT to write job postings" is probably fine. "We use an AI tool to score our applicants" is not the same thing.

**2. Understand what your vendors are doing**

Before you accepted your vendor's terms of service, did you read their section on AI? (Nobody does.) Now's the time to ask: Does this tool use AI to make decisions about individuals? What data does it use? What bias testing has been done?

**3. Get the right documents in place**

For Illinois: Employee/applicant notices, bias audit documentation, opt-out process.
For Colorado: AI governance policy, high-risk system inventory, impact assessment documentation.

**4. Don't wait for a complaint**

The most expensive version of this is reacting after someone files a charge against you. The cheapest version is having documents that show you took compliance seriously before anyone asked.

## "I'm Too Small to Get Caught"

We hear this. And look — it's probably true that you're not the attorney general's first target.

But here's the actual risk for small businesses: **private rights of action**.

[Illinois HB3773](https://www.ilga.gov/legislation/ilcs/documents/077500050K2-102.htm) allows individual employees and applicants to file charges directly through the [Illinois Department of Human Rights](https://dhr.illinois.gov/). You don't need a regulator to notice you first. One applicant who feels they were unfairly screened out by your AI system can initiate a complaint process that costs you far more in legal fees than any compliance package.

[Colorado SB24-205](https://leg.colorado.gov/bills/sb24-205) is enforced exclusively by the Colorado Attorney General — there is no private right of action under this specific statute, but AG investigations can be triggered by individual complaints. See the full penalty breakdown: [Colorado AI Law Penalties: What Non-Compliance Actually Costs](/blog/colorado-ai-law-penalties)

The math is: compliance documentation costs hundreds of dollars. Defending a complaint costs thousands, minimum, plus whatever damages get awarded.
