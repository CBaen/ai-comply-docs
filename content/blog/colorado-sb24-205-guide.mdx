June 30, 2026. That's the date. Write it somewhere.

Colorado's SB24-205 — the Colorado Artificial Intelligence Act — goes into effect on that date, and it applies to a wide range of businesses that use AI to make consequential decisions about people. If you have Colorado customers, Colorado employees, or you're headquartered in Colorado, this law is very likely relevant to you.

Here's what you actually need to know.

## What Is SB24-205?

Colorado SB24-205 is a state law regulating "high-risk artificial intelligence systems." The name sounds ominous but the concept is fairly specific: AI systems that make or substantially assist in making decisions that have a significant effect on a person's life.

We're talking about decisions around:
- Employment (hiring, firing, performance evaluations)
- Credit and financial services
- Housing
- Insurance
- Healthcare
- Education
- Legal proceedings

If you're using AI to help make those kinds of decisions about Colorado residents, you're in scope.

The law covers both **developers** (companies that build AI systems) and **deployers** (companies that use those systems in their business). Most companies reading this are deployers.

## Who Is a "Deployer" Under This Law?

A deployer is any company that puts an AI system to use — meaning you've integrated it into your products, services, or internal processes.

You don't have to have written a single line of code. You could have signed up for an AI-powered HR tool last year, or you could be using an AI credit scoring model from a fintech vendor. If that system makes consequential decisions about people, you're a deployer.

And as a deployer, you have compliance obligations.

## What Does SB24-205 Require?

Here's the plain-language version.

**1. Implement a Risk Management Program**

You need to have a written process for managing AI risk. This doesn't need to be a 200-page document, but it needs to exist and it needs to be real. Think of it as: what is your process for checking that your AI systems are doing what they're supposed to do, without causing harm?

**2. Conduct Impact Assessments**

For each high-risk AI system you deploy, you need to document:
- What the system does
- What data it uses
- What decisions it affects
- What testing has been done to check for bias or discrimination
- What your ongoing monitoring process looks like

**3. Notify Affected Individuals**

If someone is subject to a decision made (in whole or in part) by a high-risk AI system, they have to be told. Specifically, they need to know:
- That AI was used in the decision
- The nature of the data the system used
- How to request a human review

**4. Provide a Way to Appeal**

If an AI system makes or influences a decision that negatively affects someone, they have to have a way to contest it and request human review. You need a documented process for this.

**5. Maintain Records**

Documentation, documentation, documentation. The law expects you to be able to demonstrate that you did all of the above. Oral commitments don't count.

## The Affirmative Defense: The Most Important Part Nobody Talks About

Here's the provision that makes SB24-205 different from a lot of other compliance laws — and the one that makes compliance documentation especially important.

SB24-205 includes an **affirmative defense** for deployers.

What that means: if someone alleges that your AI system violated the law, you can defend yourself by demonstrating that you had a reasonable AI risk management program in place and you were using it in good faith.

In other words: having the right documentation, and actually following it, can protect you from liability — even if something goes wrong.

This is actually meaningful. It shifts the legal question from "did your AI cause harm" to "did you take reasonable steps to prevent harm." And "taking reasonable steps" is something you can document in advance.

Companies that have nothing — no policies, no assessments, no notices — can't make this argument. Companies that have done the work can.

## The Deadline: June 30, 2026

You have until June 30, 2026 to have this in place. That might feel like a while from where you're sitting. It's not.

Here's the realistic timeline for getting compliant:

- **Weeks 1-2**: Identify all AI systems in scope. Map what decisions they affect.
- **Weeks 3-4**: Conduct or obtain impact assessments for each system.
- **Weeks 5-6**: Draft your risk management policy, consumer notices, and appeal process.
- **Week 7**: Review everything, fill gaps, archive your documentation.
- **Buffer**: Leave at least 4-6 weeks before the deadline in case your AI vendor needs to provide information.

That's two to three months of actual work. If you start today, you're fine. If you start in May, you're cutting it close.

## What Are the Penalties?

The Colorado Attorney General enforces SB24-205. Civil penalties can reach up to $20,000 per violation — and each affected individual counts as a separate violation.

The math gets uncomfortable fast. If your AI system improperly affected 100 applicants in Colorado, that's potentially $2 million in exposure before legal fees.

The good news: the affirmative defense gives you a real path to mitigation if you've done the work. The bad news: "we didn't know" is not a defense.

## What Should You Do Right Now?

**1. Figure out if you have any high-risk AI systems.** If you're using AI that affects hiring, credit, insurance, housing, or healthcare for Colorado residents — the answer is probably yes.

**2. Get your impact assessments done.** Talk to your AI vendors. Get their documentation. Understand what their systems do and what testing has been done.

**3. Prepare your governance documentation.** This is the core of the affirmative defense. A written risk management policy, assessment documentation, notice templates, and an appeals process.

**4. Don't treat this as a one-time checkbox.** SB24-205 expects ongoing monitoring, not just a one-time audit. Build the process, not just the documents.
