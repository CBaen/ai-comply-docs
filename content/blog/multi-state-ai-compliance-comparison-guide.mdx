If your business operates in more than one state, there is a good chance you are already covered by multiple AI compliance laws — even if you only know about one of them.

As of early 2026, more than 15 states have enacted laws that require specific AI-related documentation, consumer notices, or bias audits from businesses operating within their borders. They don't all require the same things. They don't all have the same thresholds. And critically, they don't all apply to the same types of AI use.

This guide breaks down the eight most consequential jurisdictions for multi-state businesses: what each law requires, what makes it distinct from the others, and how to think about which ones apply to you. Every fact here is drawn directly from the enacted statute text or from prior verified coverage on this site — no educated guesses about what a law "probably" means.

## The Eight Jurisdictions That Matter Most for Multi-State Businesses

### Virginia — The Template Everyone Else Followed

Virginia was the second state in the country to enact a comprehensive consumer data privacy law, with the Virginia Consumer Data Protection Act (VCDPA) taking effect January 1, 2023. ([Va. Code §§ 59.1-575 through 59.1-584](https://law.lis.virginia.gov/vacodefull/title59.1/chapter53/))

Virginia matters for multi-state operators because it set the framework that most other state privacy laws copied: consumer thresholds (100,000 consumers processed per year, or 25,000 with 50%+ revenue from data sales), data protection assessments required for profiling and targeted advertising, Attorney General-only enforcement with no private right of action, and penalties up to $7,500 per violation. If you've built a compliance program for Virginia, you've built the foundation for Connecticut, Oregon, and several other states — because the assessment structure is substantially similar across all of them.

### Connecticut — The Cure Period Is Gone

Connecticut's CTDPA has been in effect since July 1, 2023 — nearly three years — and the mandatory 60-day cure period that originally applied expired December 31, 2024. ([Conn. Gen. Stat. §§ 42-515 through 42-525](https://www.cga.ct.gov/current/pub/chap_743jj.htm)) That means the Connecticut Attorney General now has full discretion to bring an enforcement action without first giving your business a warning.

What makes Connecticut practically useful for multi-state operators: the statute explicitly allows assessments done for Virginia, Colorado, Oregon, or the EU's GDPR to satisfy Connecticut's assessment requirement, as long as the scope and effect are reasonably comparable. ([§ 42-522](https://www.cga.ct.gov/current/pub/chap_743jj.htm#sec_42-522)) If you've already done a real assessment for another jurisdiction, you may not be starting from scratch here. The thresholds are the same as Virginia's with one difference: the 100,000 consumer count excludes data processed solely to complete a payment transaction. Penalties run up to $5,000 per violation under Connecticut's unfair trade practices statute.

### Oregon — The Cure Period Has Also Expired

Oregon's Consumer Privacy Act took effect July 1, 2024. ([ORS §§ 646A.570–646A.589](https://www.oregonlegislature.gov/bills_laws/ors/ors646A.html)) Like Connecticut, Oregon originally provided a 30-day cure period — and like Connecticut, that period expired on January 1, 2026. The Oregon AG now has enforcement discretion without a required warning first.

Oregon's threshold structure mirrors Virginia's and Connecticut's: 100,000 consumers processed per year, or 25,000 with 25%+ revenue from data sales. What distinguishes Oregon slightly is the explicit focus on profiling that presents risk of harm to consumers — the assessment requirement is triggered when profiling rises to the level of foreseeable risk, not just any automated processing. Penalties reach up to $7,500 per violation.

### Texas — Two Separate Laws, Two Separate Compliance Programs

Texas is unusual in that it has enacted two distinct AI-applicable laws that cover different things, and both are in effect.

The **Texas Data Privacy and Security Act (TDPSA)**, in effect since July 1, 2024, is Texas's consumer privacy law. ([Tex. Bus. & Com. Code Ch. 541](https://statutes.capitol.texas.gov/Docs/BC/htm/BC.541.htm)) It follows the now-familiar state privacy law structure: data protection assessments for profiling and targeted advertising, Attorney General enforcement only, no private right of action. Texas includes a permanent 30-day cure period that never expires — a meaningful difference from Connecticut and Oregon where the cure period has lapsed. Penalties are up to $7,500 per violation, but small businesses (as defined by the SBA) are generally exempt unless they sell sensitive data.

The **Texas Responsible AI Governance Act (TRAIGA, HB 149)**, in effect since January 1, 2026, is a different animal entirely. ([Tex. Gov't Code Ch. 552](https://capitol.texas.gov/BillLookup/Text.aspx?LegSess=89R&Bill=HB149)) TRAIGA covers any business that develops or deploys AI systems in Texas — there is no high-risk carveout and no small-business exemption. It requires AI inventory documentation, risk management policies, consumer disclosure, anti-discrimination controls, and correction request processes. The penalty exposure is substantially higher than any of the privacy laws: curable violations run $10,000–$12,000, uncurable violations run $80,000–$200,000, and continuing violations add $2,000–$40,000 per day on top of the base penalty. If you operate in Texas, you may need both a TDPSA assessment and a TRAIGA governance program — they address different requirements.

### Illinois — Employment-Specific, Already in Effect

Illinois HB3773 took effect January 1, 2026, amending the Illinois Human Rights Act to regulate AI in employment decisions. ([775 ILCS 5/2-102(L)](https://www.ilga.gov/legislation/ilcs/fulltext?DocName=077500050K2-102)) It applies to any employer using AI in hiring, promotion, discipline, or any other employment decision affecting Illinois employees or applicants — regardless of where the company is headquartered.

Illinois is different from the privacy laws above because it is employment-specific rather than consumer-data-focused. It doesn't require a data protection assessment in the privacy law sense; it requires notice to employees and applicants that AI is being used, plus documentation showing your AI tools aren't producing discriminatory outcomes. The penalty structure ties into the Illinois Human Rights Act's existing enforcement framework: up to $16,000 for a first violation, up to $42,500 for a second within five years, and up to $70,000 for employers with two or more prior violations within seven years, assessed per violation per aggrieved party.

The Illinois Department of Human Rights is still finalizing implementing rules on the specific format and timing of the required notice, but the underlying obligation — including the non-discrimination requirement — is fully operative now and requires no additional rulemaking to enforce.

### New York City — The Only Jurisdiction Requiring an Annual Bias Audit

NYC Local Law 144 has been in effect since July 5, 2023, and it is the only jurisdiction in this comparison that requires an annual third-party bias audit. ([NYC Admin. Code § 20-870 et seq.](https://www.nyc.gov/site/dca/about/automated-employment-decision-tools.page)) The law applies to employers and employment agencies that use automated employment decision tools (AEDTs) to screen candidates or employees for hiring or promotion in New York City.

An AEDT is any computational process using machine learning, statistical modeling, data analytics, or AI that produces a score, classification, or recommendation used to substantially assist or replace discretionary decision making in hiring. The audit must be conducted by an independent third party, must cover intersectional bias across race/ethnicity and sex categories as required by DCWP implementing rules, and a public summary must be posted on the employer's website at least ten business days before the tool is used on any candidate. Penalties start at $500 for a first violation and $500–$1,500 per subsequent violation per day — but each use of a non-audited tool on each candidate constitutes a separate potential violation.

### California — The Largest Market, the Most Layers

California's compliance structure is the most complex because it has two distinct compliance tracks with different deadlines.

California's CCPA-based ADMT regulations, finalized by the California Office of Administrative Law on September 22, 2025, create staggered obligations: risk assessments for qualifying processing activities were required as of January 1, 2026; consumer-facing opt-out rights and pre-use notices for automated decision-making technology are required by January 1, 2027; and attestation submissions to CalPrivacy are due by April 1, 2028. ([Cal. Civ. Code § 1798.100 et seq.](https://cppa.ca.gov/regulations/ccpa_updates.html))

The CCPA applies to businesses doing business in California that meet any one of three thresholds: annual gross revenue over $25 million, processing personal information of 100,000 or more consumers or households per year, or deriving 50%+ of annual revenue from selling or sharing personal information. That $25 million gross revenue threshold is based on company-wide revenue, not California-specific revenue. CalPrivacy has demonstrated active enforcement: it fined Tractor Supply Company $1.35 million in September 2025 — the largest fine in the agency's history — for CCPA violations. ([According to CalPrivacy's September 30, 2025 announcement](https://cppa.ca.gov/announcements/2025/20250930.html).) The ADMT penalty baseline is $2,500 per unintentional violation and $7,500 per intentional violation — and when you process data for thousands of consumers, those numbers multiply.

### Colorado — High-Risk AI, The Most Detailed Deployer Requirements

Colorado's SB 24-205 takes effect June 30, 2026, making it the nearest upcoming deadline for any state in this comparison. ([C.R.S. § 6-1-1701 et seq.](https://leg.colorado.gov/bills/sb24-205)) It is also the most structurally demanding law for businesses that deploy AI in high-stakes decisions.

Colorado focuses specifically on "high-risk" AI systems — those that make or substantially factor into "consequential decisions" affecting a consumer's access to employment, education, financial services, housing, insurance, healthcare, or legal services. Both developers (companies that build AI systems) and deployers (companies that use them) have separate obligations. Deployers must implement a risk management program, conduct impact assessments for each high-risk system, provide consumer notice before or at the time of a consequential decision, offer consumers the right to correct data and appeal adverse decisions, and make a public statement about their AI governance practices.

Colorado's law provides an affirmative defense for businesses that comply with a nationally recognized AI risk management framework — the NIST AI Risk Management Framework is the most prominent reference. Penalties are enforced under the Colorado Consumer Protection Act at up to $20,000 per violation (and up to $50,000 per violation when the affected consumer is age 60 or older). If you're already preparing for Colorado, the impact assessment structure closely parallels what Virginia, Connecticut, and Oregon require for consumer data profiling.

## What These Laws Have in Common

Across all eight jurisdictions, three patterns hold consistently.

**Documentation is the primary obligation.** No state AI law reviewed here requires businesses to stop using AI. They require businesses to document that they've evaluated the risks, notified affected people, and built processes to catch discriminatory outcomes. The documentation is both the compliance artifact and the legal defense.

**Attorney General enforcement is universal; private lawsuits are rare.** None of the consumer privacy laws or AI governance laws in this comparison create a general private right of action for AI-specific violations. Enforcement runs through state AGs. Illinois HB3773 is the notable exception: it ties AI violations into the Illinois Human Rights Act's existing enforcement framework, which does allow private civil actions with uncapped actual damages.

**Penalties scale with automated systems.** Most of these laws set penalties on a per-violation basis. When an AI system makes hundreds or thousands of decisions per day, "per violation" compounds quickly. A hiring platform screening 500 resumes a day in a non-compliant way faces a different risk calculation than a business making ten manual decisions a month.

## Which States Apply to You? A Decision Framework

Use this framework to narrow down your actual exposure.

**Start with what your AI does, not where you're headquartered.**

If your AI affects **employment decisions** — hiring, promotion, discipline, termination — check Illinois HB3773 (any Illinois employees or applicants), NYC Local Law 144 (any NYC hiring), and Colorado SB 24-205 (any consequential decisions about Colorado employees, effective June 30, 2026).

If your AI affects **consumer data** — profiling, targeted advertising, personalization, scoring — check whether you meet the consumer data thresholds in any of the following: Virginia (100K consumers or 25K with 50%+ data revenue), Connecticut (100K excluding payment-only, or 25K with 25%+ data revenue), Oregon (100K or 25K with 25%+ data revenue), Texas TDPSA (same thresholds, small business exemption applies), and California (25M gross revenue, OR 100K consumers, OR 50%+ revenue from data sales).

If your AI is **deployed or developed in Texas** regardless of what it does — Texas TRAIGA applies to any AI system with no high-risk carveout and no minimum size threshold.

If you serve **more than one of these states**, the core assessment documents overlap substantially. Virginia, Connecticut, Oregon, and the TDPSA all use a similar data protection assessment structure, and Connecticut's statute explicitly says a compliant assessment for one can satisfy another. A multi-state assessment built for all four at once is more efficient than four separate documents.
